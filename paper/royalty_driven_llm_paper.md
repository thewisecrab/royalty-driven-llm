## 1. Introduction

The core economic complaint about generative AI is simple: models gain commercial
value from the accumulated work of human creators, while creators often receive no
ongoing compensation. Joseph Gordon-Levitt's Sundance discussion of AI and creator
rights frames this as a missing infrastructure problem: if a model output can be
connected back to influential inputs, then platforms can pay creators in a recurring
fashion rather than relying only on one-time buyouts or litigation.

Video platforms demonstrate one related economic structure. YouTube's Partner
Program shares advertising and subscription revenue with creators, including a 55%
share for long-form ad revenue and 45% for Shorts revenue according to YouTube's
2025 explanation of the program [@youtube2025ypp]. YouTube Content ID also shows
that large-scale rights identification can be built into a platform workflow, with
matches leading to blocking, monetization, revenue sharing, or tracking
[@youtubeContentID].

AI systems require a different accounting unit. A video view is not the same as a
model generation, API call, agent action, or enterprise answer. The proposed unit is
therefore a monetized AI usage event: a prompt or task, retrieved registered sources,
model output, text matches, citation evidence, source-support scores, payout weights,
and an event hash. RDLLM-L144 adds a universal confidential attribution audit so
private training, post-training, serving, citation, valuation, revocation, and
creator-query evidence can support attribution and royalty claims through
commitments, proof handles, and auditor quorum without exposing raw corpora,
prompts, answers, customer logs, source text, reward data, or model internals.

provider must prove actor, intent, context, tool, model-invocation, inference,

## 2. Related Work

Data valuation has a growing machine-learning literature. Ghorbani and Zou propose
Data Shapley to quantify the value of individual training data points to model
performance and argue that it satisfies natural properties of equitable valuation
[@ghorbani2019data]. Koh and Liang use influence functions to trace model predictions
back through training data [@koh2017influence]. Pruthi et al. introduce TracIn, a
gradient-checkpoint method for estimating training-data influence [@pruthi2020tracin].
DataDignity frames training-data attribution as pinpoint provenance for LLM outputs,
ranking candidate documents that support a response [@li2026datadignity]. DebugLM
learns provenance tags that trace LLM behavior to responsible datasets and supports
source-specific remediation [@mo2026debuglm]. OLMoTrace shows that large-scale
open-data language models can expose real-time output-to-training-corpus matches
[@liu2025olmotrace]. DATE-LM benchmarks attribution methods across training-data
selection, toxicity/bias filtering, and factual attribution, finding task-specific
trade-offs rather than a universal best method [@jiao2025datelm]. Recent LLM-scale
influence-function work further argues that data valuation can be scaled to modern
LLM datasets, though at significant compute cost [@choe2025dataworth]. RISE
compresses output-layer influence evidence for retrospective attribution and
prospective data valuation, making model-native attribution more plausible for
deployment-scale systems [@ran2026rise]. LoRIF further reduces storage and
query-time costs for influence-function attribution at large model scale
[@li2026lorif]. MaxShapley targets fair, incentive-compatible context attribution
for generative search compensation, strengthening the case for payout weights that
are grounded in retrieved-source utility rather than only display frequency
[@patel2026maxshapley].
Concept Influence attributes behavior to semantic directions rather than only
single examples, which makes it relevant for diffuse training-value evidence that
does not map cleanly to one cited document [@kowal2026conceptInfluence]. ZK-Value
shows that data valuation can be made verifiable without exposing private
valuation inputs by combining locality-sensitive Shapley valuation with
zero-knowledge proof techniques [@wang2026zkvalue]. RDLLM treats these methods as
valuation evidence channels for residual corpus royalties, but keeps rights
gating, visible-footer attribution, direct-answer payout, residual-pool payout,
and escrow as separate surfaces.
Recent 2026 attribution work reinforces the same design constraint from several
angles: RAG attribution needs a unified hallucination/failure taxonomy
[@zhao2026ragAttributionSurvey], zeroth-order and uncertainty-based data
attribution seek cheaper influence estimates for LLMs [@kokhlikyan2026z0inf;
@pan2026daunce], and rescaled influence functions address high-dimensional
underestimation in standard influence methods [@rubinstein2026rif]. RDLLM treats
these methods as evidence channels that must be bound to receipts, source footers,
clearing, and remittance rather than as standalone payout oracles.
Recent 2026 work widens the evidence surface beyond lexical retrieval. Probing for
Knowledge Attribution shows that hidden-state probes can classify the dominant
knowledge source behind an output [@brink2026probing]. Mechanistic Data Attribution
uses influence functions to trace interpretable units back to training samples
[@chen2026mda]. Probabilistic token attribution estimates which prior tokens most
shape a response from next-token probabilities and entropy [@shilpika2026probattr].
Attribution Bias in Large Language Models introduces "suppression" as a failure
mode where systems omit attribution even when authorship information is available
[@berman2026attributionBias]. RDLLM therefore names attribution suppression in
answer cards and source-confidence reports rather than treating omitted known
sources as a generic citation failure.
Recent agent-runtime work shows why attribution cannot stop at a footer or payout
table. The Sovereign Context Protocol proposes attribution-aware access to
creator-owned data [@panchigar2026scp], HDP signs multi-hop human delegation in
agentic systems [@dalugoda2026hdp], PROV-AGENT extends provenance into MCP-style
agent workflows [@souza2025provAgent], production MCP analysis identifies missing
identity propagation and observability primitives [@srinivasan2026mcpProduction],
and explicit-provenance work argues that responsibility for agentic AI must be
computable and interventionable [@hu2026responsibleAgenticProvenance]. RDLLM-L145
therefore treats source attribution, tool use, memory use, model invocation,
footer release, and settlement as one authorized runtime chain.
RDLLM-L146 then turns that runtime chain into a provider-neutral root by combining
explicit provenance, CUE-R evidence utility [@jain2026cuer], faithful citation
attribution [@choi2025citeguard], AIBOM lifecycle assurance [@hu2026aibom],
SCITT-style transparent statements [@ietf2026scitt], W3C Data Integrity
[@w3cDataIntegrity], and C2PA content credentials [@c2pa2026spec] into one public
verification object.
RDLLM-L147 turns that root into an emission rule by combining deep-research
citation parsing and evaluation [@onweller2026citedNotVerified],
argument-level provenance for agent enforcement [@fan2026pact],
runtime protection controllers [@liu2026safeagent], verifiable training/release
attestations [@tan2026attestingPipelines], Warrant Certificate Authorities
[@bondar2026wca], cryptographic runtime governance receipts
[@brennan2026runtimeGovernance], DAVinCI dual attribution/verification
[@rawte2026davinci], and OpenTelemetry GenAI semantic conventions
[@opentelemetryGenAI2026] into one fail-closed response-emission proof.
RDLLM-L148 turns the proof stack into a deployable provider profile by combining
verified citation footers [@onweller2026citedNotVerified], RAG source
attribution [@nematov2025sourceAttributionRag], mechanistic data attribution
[@chen2026mda], SCP-style creator access logs
[@panchigar2026scp], SourceTracker-style generated-code provenance
[@gurioli2026sourcetracker], OpenTelemetry GenAI [@opentelemetryGenAI2026],
C2PA 2.4 [@c2pa2026spec], SCITT/RFC 9943 [@ietf2026scitt], W3C Data Integrity

The cross-provider API mapping follows the same portability pressure described
by LLM-Rosetta, which uses a hub-and-spoke IR for request, response, tool, and

RDLLM-L149 then turns the provider profile into a runtime receipt by combining

RDLLM-L150 then moves attribution from route conformance to claim-level display:

This operationalizes TRACER's verifiable generative provenance
[@yu2026tracer], AgentSim's verifiable agent-trace simulation

retrieved documents in RAG while studying lower-cost approximations

[@qin2026cfrag], while CoRM-RAG frames counterfactual intervention as a way to align
retrieval with decision safety rather than semantic relevance alone
[@liu2026cormrag]. RDLLM maps this line of work into a practical provider artifact:
source-ranking benchmarks test whether the right sources are found, and
counterfactual influence reports test whether credited sources still matter under
source-removal replay.

and citation-support metrics [@martin2025mirage]. Social-media attribution work also studies signed identifiers

maps these findings into a signed media attribution report with content hashes,
perceptual-hash commitments, descriptor-hash commitments, ranked match scores,
owner payouts, and unattributed-media escrow.

not merely plausible. Cited but Not Verified evaluates deep-research reports by link
availability, topical relevance, and factual support, showing that strong link
validity can coexist with much weaker fact support [@onweller2026cited]. CiteVQA's
Strict Attributed Accuracy credits an answer only when both the answer and cited
evidence region are correct, exposing attribution hallucination in document
intelligence systems [@ma2026citevqa]. RDLLM therefore binds visible footers to
source-verification reports and keeps model-signal attribution separate from public
citation display. DAVinCI similarly combines attribution to internal/external
evidence with entailment-based verification and confidence calibration, which
supports RDLLM's split between source confidence and the final footer-rendering
contract [@rawte2026davinci].
The same evidence motivates a separate semantic text-attribution surface:
DataDignity's FakeWiki benchmark weakens lexical shortcuts with paraphrases,
retro-generated variants, and hard anti-documents [@li2026datadignity], while
evidence-ranking work argues that sufficient evidence must be exposed early enough
for human verification [@alt2026evidenceRanking]. RDLLM therefore adds a
`rdllm-semantic-text-attribution/v1` report that ranks paraphrased or external
outputs against registered sources, publishes source footer rows, and escrows weak
or ambiguous semantic matches.

Generated code needs a separate path because source reuse can trigger license
obligations even when the answer is not prose. HybridSourceTracker shows that
large-scale code provenance can combine vector retrieval with exact fingerprints to
trace generated snippets back to likely training examples [@gurioli2026sourcetracker].
RDLLM therefore adds `rdllm-code-attribution-report/v1`: a privacy-safe report over
generated-code hashes, line-hash roots, token and identifier commitments, ranked
source snippets, SPDX-style license compatibility, owner payouts, and escrow for
incompatible copied-code signals.

Provenance standards provide a second foundation. W3C PROV defines provenance as
information about entities, activities, and people involved in producing a data item
or thing, useful for assessing reliability and trustworthiness [@w3c2013prov]. C2PA
develops technical standards for certifying the source and history of media content
[@c2pa2026spec]. W3C Verifiable Credentials 2.0 supplies a standardized
issuer/subject/proof model for portable credentials and presentations, which is a
natural fit for ownership attestations over registered works and for exporting
runtime attribution claims [@w3cVc2025].
Recent C2PA security analysis argues that content-provenance specifications should
not be relied on alone for high-stakes media trust [@golaszewski2026c2paShortfall].
Recent provenance-and-watermarking work makes the same point operationally: durable
provenance needs a threat model for laundering, cross-model regeneration,
watermark removal, and insider forgery, not only a metadata container
[@lundstromimanov2026provenanceWatermarking].
RDLLM therefore treats media provenance as an input to a replayable economic proof,
not as a substitute for attribution, payout conservation, and escrow verification.
Payment messaging standards provide the last-mile remittance shape. ISO 20022
Payments Initiation messages define structured payment instructions and remittance
information for reconciliation [@iso20022paymentsInitiation]. RDLLM borrows that
shape for instruction-only payment rows while withholding raw creator account
details from public artifacts. RDLLM then adds a separate execution attestation:
processor or escrow settlement records must be reduced to hash-only rows and
matched back to the remittance file before a provider may describe creator value as
paid, settled, or escrowed.

Supply-chain transparency standards provide the deployability pattern. IETF SCITT
defines signed statements, transparency services, and receipts for trustworthy
digital supply chains and is in RFC-to-be 9943 final review in 2026
[@ietf2026scitt].
Certificate Transparency v2 specifies append-only Merkle logs and inclusion proofs
for public monitoring, a pattern RDLLM adapts from certificates to attribution
proof surfaces [@ietf2021ct].
The C2PA conformance program demonstrates a practical path from provenance
specification to vetted generator and validator product behavior
[@c2paConformance]. Sigstore/Rekor demonstrates practical
transparency logs with signed artifact metadata and inclusion proofs [@sigstoreRekor].
Key-discovery and trust-root standards supply the complementary signer layer:
JSON Web Key sets support key identifiers and key rollover [@ietf2015jwk], W3C DID
Core defines verification methods for decentralized identifiers [@w3cDidCore],
and Sigstore documents a TUF-distributed root of trust for signing infrastructure
[@sigstoreSecurity; @sigstoreCosignOverview]. RDLLM maps those patterns into a
provider-neutral trust registry for attribution proof keys.
The transparency-dev witness project adds witness cosigning for log checkpoints,
which RDLLM adapts as an anti-equivocation control over attribution-publication
monitors [@transparencyDevWitness].
The in-toto/SLSA attestation pattern shows how production metadata can be made
verifiable across toolchains [@intotoAttestations]. SPDX 3.0 extends bill-of-materials
concepts to AI systems and model artifacts [@spdx301], while MLCommons Croissant
standardizes machine-readable ML dataset metadata, provenance, and permissions
[@mlcommonsCroissant].
W3C ODRL supplies a rights-expression model for permissions, prohibitions, duties,
and constraints over content and services [@w3cOdrl]. Croissant 1.1 further
emphasizes structured usage policies and machine-actionable provenance for automated
consent and licensing enforcement [@mlcommonsCroissant11].
C2PA's 2026 TDM clarification is also instructive: content credentials record
provenance and authenticity, but they are not a rights-management protocol for
text-and-data-mining permissions [@c2paTdmClarification]. RDLLM therefore keeps
provenance, rights policy, telemetry, and settlement separate but cryptographically
linked.
OpenAI's May 2026 provenance update shows major model providers moving toward
multi-layer provenance, conforming generator products, durable watermarking, and
public verification tooling [@openai2026provenance]. The Sovereign Context Protocol
argues for runtime access logs where LLM access to creator-owned data is licensed
and attributable by default [@panchigar2026scp]. RDLLM's provider attribution card
turns those deployment concerns into a signed, comparable disclosure artifact that
can be checked against receipts, ledgers, and certification reports.

Selective-disclosure standards provide the privacy pattern needed for public
receipts. W3C Verifiable Credentials 2.0 defines selective disclosure as
fine-grained control over shared information [@w3cVc2025]. IETF RFC 9901 specifies
SD-JWT, where issuer-signed JSON claims are verified through selected disclosures,
digests, and high-entropy salts [@ietfSdJwt9901]. W3C Data Integrity 1.0 describes
selectively disclosing parts of a previously signed message without disturbing the
verifiability of the disclosed parts [@w3cDataIntegrity]. The W3C BBS cryptosuite
draft provides a migration path toward unlinkable derived proofs for VC-shaped
credentials [@w3cBbs2026].

Production observability standards supply the provider-log bridge. OpenTelemetry's
GenAI semantic conventions define provider-neutral spans and attributes for model
operations, data-source IDs, and structured retrieval documents
[@otelGenaiSemconv]. Strauss et al. argue that LLM search systems need end-to-end
search traces covering query, retrieval, reranking, and citation to measure the
gap between content consumed and content cited [@strauss2026attributionCrisis].
CUE-R further shows that answer-only evaluation misses evidence-item utility in
RAG and that intervention traces can expose which retrieved items matter
[@jain2026cuer]. TRACER identifies a provenance gap in multimodal tool-using
agents: tool trajectories and final answers often fail to bind each generated
claim to the observation that supports it [@yu2026tracer]. RDLLM's trace exchange
adapts those conventions for royalty accountability, and its portable attribution
capsule binds the copied answer back to the public proof chain after the response
leaves the original API boundary.

Recent attributed-generation work motivates the user-facing source footer. AIS
defines whether generated statements are attributable to identified sources
[@rashkin2023ais]. ALCE evaluates generated answers along correctness and citation
quality and shows that citation support remains incomplete in difficult long-form
settings [@gao2023alce]. RECLAIM proposes sentence-level citations through
interleaved reference-claim generation [@xia2025reclaim], while Think&Cite treats
attributed generation as search guided by generation and attribution rewards
[@li2025thinkcite]. C2-Cite makes citation markers active source pointers rather
than generic bracket tokens [@yu2026c2cite].
FRAMES evaluates factuality, retrieval, and reasoning as one RAG task
[@krishna2025frames], and the TREC 2025 RAG Track explicitly evaluates attribution
verification in end-to-end systems [@upadhyay2026trecRag].
VeriCite adds claim verification, supporting-evidence selection, and answer
refinement for reliable citations [@qian2025vericite].
SURE-RAG treats evidence sufficiency as a set-level decision over coverage,
relation strength, disagreement, conflict, and retrieval uncertainty, which aligns
with RDLLM's decision to separate source availability, sufficient support, and
counterevidence adjudication rather than treating retrieval as proof
[@qiu2026surerag].
PaperTrail shows that document-level citations are too coarse for scholarly
verification and motivates claim-evidence mappings that expose support, omission,
and unsupported assertions [@martinboyle2026papertrail].
SAFE adds a sentence-level pre-attribution step for deciding whether claims should
cite no source, a single quote, or multiple quotes [@batista2025safe].

Recent diagnostics reinforce the need for verification after retrieval. RAGChecker
offers fine-grained retrieval and generation diagnostics [@ru2024ragchecker].
MedRAGChecker decomposes biomedical answers into atomic claims and checks evidence
support [@ji2026medragchecker]. LUMINA shows that RAG systems can still hallucinate
when models underuse external context and overuse internal knowledge [@yeh2026lumina].
Wallat et al. show that citation correctness is not enough because post-rationalized
citations may support statements without faithfully reflecting source reliance
[@wallat2025faithfulness].
Zhao et al. show that hallucinated citations are already appearing in scholarly
literature at large scale [@zhao2026wildcitations].
GhostCite similarly reports broad citation-generation hallucination rates and a
human verification gap, strengthening the case for machine-checkable citation
identity, metadata, and evidence support [@xu2026ghostcite].
Li, Lin, and Ma's 2026 CiteTracer work adds that citation verification should be
field-level and taxonomy-aligned, because binary found/not-found checks miss
actionable metadata drift and fabricated-citation modes [@li2026citetracer].
Saxena et al. compare generation-time and post-hoc citation and find a practical
coverage-correctness trade-off [@saxena2025citation]. Recent LLM search work
documents an attribution gap between web pages consumed and sources credited
[@attributionCrisis2025]. Ponnuraj's 2026 retrieval-jitter study shows that
document-level overlap can obscure span-level evidence instability, motivating exact
span hashes in audit artifacts [@ponnuraj2026jitter]. CiteGuard reframes citation
quality as citation-attribution alignment and validates citations through
retrieval-aware checking [@choi2025citeguard]. CiteCheck verifies whether generated
scientific citations point to real works and whether metadata is faithful to the
retrieved candidate, reinforcing the need for citation footers to resolve to
external evidence rather than model-written strings [@khajavi2026citecheck].
CiteAudit similarly decomposes scholarly citation verification into metadata
extraction, retrieval, passage matching, reasoning, and calibrated judgment
[@shi2026citeaudit]. Alt et al.'s user-centric evidence ranking work argues that
verification systems should present sufficient evidence early enough for human
inspection rather than relying on redundant snippets [@alt2026evidenceRanking].
SciTrue links scientific claim components to explicit, inspectable sources and
reports stronger traceability than general-purpose search-augmented systems
[@tan2026scitrue].
Prompt-injection work adds a second constraint: the source text used for grounding
must not become a control channel. OWASP LLM01:2025 treats direct and indirect
prompt injection as a primary LLM application risk and recommends segregating
external content [@owasp2025llm01]. Chaturvedi et al. show that reusable
instructional prompts can be weaponized to manipulate RAG behavior
[@chaturvedi2025aip]. Khodayari et al. document indirect prompt injections in
webpages and HTTP responses at web scale [@khodayari2026indirectWild]. RDLLM
therefore adds source-boundary reports: source packets may support claims, but
signed telemetry must prove they were evidence-only data, not instructions or
policy-mutating input.
Recent poisoning work adds a third constraint: the source itself can be malicious,
synthetic, or origin-laundered even when it is retrievable and topically relevant.
Poisoned-MRAG shows that a few injected image-text pairs can steer multimodal RAG
outputs [@liu2025poisonedMrag], RAGOrigin frames poisoned generations as a
responsibility-attribution problem over candidate retrieved texts
[@zhang2025ragorigin], and M3Att shows query-agnostic poisoning in medical
multimodal RAG [@yang2026m3att]. RDLLM therefore adds source-authenticity reports:
direct credit and direct payout require trusted origin evidence, active license
terms, archive consensus, synthetic-source disclosure, and low poisoning and
source-farm risk.
Onweller et al. evaluate LLM
deep-research citations by checking whether links work, cited content is relevant,
and facts are supported; their 2026 results show that high link validity can coexist
with much lower factual support, which supports RDLLM's decision to bind citation
labels to claim-level evidence and verifier checks [@onweller2026cited].
SourceBench similarly argues that AI-cited source quality should be scored along
content relevance, factual accuracy, objectivity, freshness, accountability, and
clarity rather than treated as a binary citation marker [@jin2026sourcebench].
Recent empirical work on Google AI Overviews, Google Search, and Gemini makes the
same issue visible at web-search scale: activation patterns, source quality,
claim fidelity, publisher traffic effects, and source reuse are ecosystem-level
questions rather than UI details [@xu2026aio; @wu2026aioEcosystem;
@grossman2026generativeSearch]. RDLLM therefore treats answer footers as
auditable distribution and confidence surfaces, not as decorative citations.
LegalCiteBench shows that citation verification failures are acute in legal
settings, where models can produce concrete but incorrect authorities
[@chen2026legalcitebench]. Geissler et al. argue for factual confidence prediction
in RAG systems so retrieved chunks are assessed for whether they actually support
the generated answer [@geissler2026dependableRag].
Rai et al.'s 2025 royalty framework for AI-generated art independently argues that
influence tracing can support transparent attribution and equitable royalty
distribution [@rai2025royaltyArt]. Bozenhard's 2026 discussion of inspirational
responsibility highlights why AI training restrictions and attribution duties cannot
be reduced to ordinary human learning analogies [@bozenhard2026inspirational].

Policy signals are also converging. The EU AI Act requires providers of general-purpose
AI models to maintain a copyright policy and publish a sufficiently detailed summary
of training content [@eu2025gpai; @eu2024aiAct]. The European Commission's 2026 FAQ
states that the public summary template is mandatory under Article 53(1)(d) and
should be updated at six-month intervals or sooner for material changes
[@ec2026trainingTemplate]. The U.S. Copyright Office's 2025 report on
generative AI training analyzes consent, compensation, voluntary licensing, collective
licensing, and statutory approaches [@usco2025training]. Nature Machine
Intelligence's 2025 editorial argues that training-data transparency should be a
starting point for resolving generative-AI copyright disputes
[@nature2025transparency]. Samuelson's analysis of collective licensing emphasizes
the practical difficulty of compensating copyright owners at generative-AI scale
[@samuelson2026collective], while Peukert's economics model argues for broad
coverage, low administrative cost, and the smallest royalty that sustains fresh
creative supply [@peukert2025economics]. Creative-worker interviews
reported by Kyi et al. show that consent, credit, and compensation are central creator
concerns [@kyi2025governance]. Lanier and Weyl's data dignity proposal supplies the
broader economic intuition that people whose data contributes value should participate
in that value [@lanier2018blueprint].
IPTC's opt-out best practices show that rights reservations can be expressed
through metadata, robots/TDMRep/trust.txt signals, and asset-level declarations, but
also warn that declaration protocols do not themselves prove crawler or model
compliance [@iptc2025optout]. RDLLM therefore treats consent as a lifecycle:
updated owner intent must produce a verifiable future-use behavior change.
Privacy-preserving finance and audit research adds the corresponding accounting
constraint. PrivaRisk uses commitments and verifiable private-set intersection for
auditable financial data sharing [@lei2025privarisk], while PrivChain-AI combines
permissioned ledgers, federated learning, and privacy-preserving financial
reporting controls [@privchainai2026]. RDLLM adopts the same design direction for
creator royalties: public artifacts should prove reconciliation and traceability,
but the public proof surface should contain hashes and rollups rather than raw
customer, invoice, payment, prompt, output, or source text.

## 3. Mechanism Design

A Royalty-Driven LLM has the following layers:

1. Registry: creators register works, license terms, payout accounts, and content hashes.
2. Ownership-claim trust: duplicate and near-duplicate registrations across creators
   are detected before settlement, with open conflicts routed to escrow.
3. Rights policy: allowed uses, prohibited uses, jurisdiction, revocation, and minimum
   creator-pool rates are evaluated before attribution.
4. Retrieval and generation: the model answers using registered sources where possible.
5. Text attribution: generated outputs are matched against registered text even when
   the output came from an external AI system.
6. Claim grounding: answer sentences are checked against candidate source chunks,
   linked to evidence spans, and rendered with a source footer.
7. Grounding quality: the event scores source accessibility, citation integrity,
   evidence relevance, fact support, policy alignment, and payout alignment.
8. Source-access accountability: retrieval and text-match paths record every source
   considered for response use with scores, ranks, rights status, and registry
   status.
9. Attribution-gap reporting: accessed sources must reconcile with visible source
   footers, paid royalty shares, or explicit policy/registry escrow.
10. Attribution: the system scores each source's contribution to the event.
11. Receipting: the event emits a signed attribution receipt and optional public
   receipt.
12. Selective disclosure: public packages disclose source and claim-support facts
    while salted commitments bind private prompts, evidence text, access traces,
    rights decisions, registry decisions, and payouts.
13. Trace exchange: provider telemetry exports OpenTelemetry-aligned generation,
    source-access, citation, and claim-support spans bound to the receipt.
14. Transparency: receipt hashes can be appended to a Merkle transparency log.
15. Interoperability: receipts and settlements export as VC-shaped credentials, and
   each answer exports as a PROV-shaped provenance graph.
16. Dispute release: resolved ownership conflicts release registry-dispute escrow
   through verifiable settlement reports.
17. Settlement: a creator pool is split according to contribution weights and written to
   an audit ledger.
18. Aggregate royalty statements: periodic public reports roll many events into
   creator, escrow, work, source-usage, receipt, and trace commitments without
   exposing prompts, outputs, source quotes, evidence text, or matched text.
19. Attribution challenges: creators can contest an omitted or weakly attributed
   source through a signed correction report that binds the original event and
   challenged content hash without rewriting the event.
20. Derivative lineage reports: source payouts for summaries, synthetic datasets,
   tool outputs, and transformed corpora can recursively split between immediate
   derivative owners and upstream registered owners.
21. Provenance evaluation reports: providers replay portable source-attribution
   benchmarks across clean, paraphrased, hard-decoy, unattributed-escrow, and
   derivative-lineage cases without disclosing benchmark prompts, outputs, or
   source text.
22. Counterfactual influence reports: providers remove each credited work, replay
   the same prompt and answer, and publish source-removal status, payout
   reallocation or escrow status, substitute-source pressure, and impact margins.
23. Media attribution reports: providers match image, audio, video, 3D, and
   text-shaped media signatures to registered assets, publish hash commitments and
   ranked scores, pay matched owners, and escrow weak or unknown media influence.
24. Model signal attribution reports: providers verify private log-probability,
   activation, gradient, attention, and memorization signals through explicit
   attribution contracts, scalar score rows, payout shares, and escrow.
25. Rights remediation reports: providers compare previous and updated rights
   states, preserve historical event hashes, and prove future denied use after
   revocation, opt-out, license, or policy changes.
26. Semantic text attribution reports: providers rank paraphrased, summarized, or
   external text against registered sources, publish source footer rows, pay
   accepted owners, and escrow ambiguous or unmatched text.
27. Provider attribution cards: model providers publish certification level,
   coverage metrics, supported evidence channels, public disclosure surfaces,
   challenge policy, limitations, and privacy-safe evidence roots.
28. Training-content summaries and residual corpus royalty reports: model providers
   publish GPAI/Croissant/SPDX/ODRL aligned summaries of training-corpus
   categories, rights coverage, license counts, policy roots, content-hash roots,
   and training-value commitments without disclosing work text; when model usage
   creates diffuse licensed training-corpus value, a separate residual report
   binds revenue rows, creator-license terms, valuation evidence hashes,
   direct-attribution exclusions, creator caps, payable rows, escrow rows, and
   creator receipts without replacing the visible source footer. A companion
   valuation-method audit binds those residual rows to method-code commitments,
   benchmark-suite commitments, known-contributor checks, hard anti-document
   rejections, duplicate guards, calibration rows, stability rows, and privacy or
   zero-knowledge proof commitments.
29. Answer provenance cards: users and downstream systems receive a compact,
    signed card that binds the visible source footer, claim span hashes, receipt
    hash, trace hash, grounding verdicts, and attribution-gap verdict without
    disclosing private prompt or evidence text.
30. Source verification reports: providers publish hash-only materialization reports
    proving cited sources, quote hashes, source URIs, claim evidence spans, footer
    span prefixes, and answer-card bindings resolve to registered content without
    disclosing private source or evidence text.
31. Response envelopes: providers return signed API packages containing the rendered
    answer, answer-card, source-verification, source-confidence,
    creator-license, public-receipt, provider-card, and certification artifacts so
    customers can verify the response boundary.
32. Integration profiles: providers publish signed API contracts containing
    endpoints, required headers, embedded artifact requirements, verifier commands,
    schema maps, public surfaces, readiness checks, and bound proof hashes for
    RDLLM-compatible model APIs.
33. Assurance bundles: providers publish hash-only proof-pack summaries with Merkle
    roots and inclusion proofs for receipts, traces, statements, challenges,
    answer cards, source verification reports, source confidence reports,
    response envelopes, integration profiles, provider cards, training summaries,
    derivative-lineage reports,
    provenance-evaluation reports, counterfactual-influence reports,
    media-attribution reports, model-signal reports, rights-remediation reports,
    semantic-text-attribution reports, revenue allocation reports, finance ledger
    attestations, and
    certification reports.
34. Discovery manifests: providers publish a signed `/.well-known/rdllm.json`
    entry point that catalogs public proof paths, artifact hashes, API contract
    hashes, schema maps, verifier commands, readiness checks, and bound proof
    hashes for customers, creators, auditors, and downstream platforms.
35. Attribution exchange manifests: downstream AI providers import upstream
    provider-card, certification, integration-profile, discovery-manifest,
    response-envelope, assurance-bundle, and semantic-text proofs by hash while
    preserving public source-footer rows and escrow obligations.
36. Conformance vector packs: independent implementers receive fixture hashes,
    expected outcomes, verifier commands, and negative mutation cases that make
    compatibility claims reproducible before market adoption.
37. Runtime federation handshakes: providers negotiate a minimum attribution level,
    artifact hashes, source-footer relay, escrow relay, runtime headers,
    signatures, and downgrade rejection before an upstream proof or answer is
    consumed by a downstream AI system.
38. Portable attribution capsules: providers publish a signed copy/repost proof
    that binds the rendered-output hash to the response envelope, federation
    handshake, attribution exchange, conformance vector pack, copyable footer
    marker, delivered-body hash, runtime capsule headers, C2PA-compatible assertion,
    and SCITT-like statement subject without exposing private prompt, answer,
    source, matched, or hidden-state text.
39. Response release gates: providers block answer emission unless the response
    envelope, source materialization report, attribution capsule, provider public
    surface, and minimum upstream certification all verify.
40. Proof-carrying responses: providers deliver the answer and its verification
    evidence together, suppressing the original answer whenever the release gate
    fails.
41. Serving gateway reports: providers prove the API egress route delivered exactly
    the proof-carrying response copied output while only hash-committing the
    private prompt and raw model output.
42. Creator license contracts: providers publish hash-bound, signed terms that
    connect each registered work to allowed uses, attribution duties,
    compensation duties, standards metadata, payout commitments, and privacy
    guarantees before model training, retrieval, generation, or federation use.
43. Source confidence reports: providers publish signed public footer-confidence
    reports that combine answer provenance, source materialization, and creator
    license terms into verified/warning/failed source rows, claim rows, and
    hallucination taxonomy.
44. Citation footer contracts: providers publish signed client-rendering contracts
    that bind exact source rows, claim anchors, confidence labels, license status,
    royalty status, display order, row hashes, and footer hash to the response
    proofs before any client displays the source footer.
45. Private audit challenges: providers publish auditor-nonce-bound commitments
    over selected redacted receipt paths so authorized auditors can verify hidden
    source-access, claim-evidence, rights, registry, and payout facts without
    public disclosure of prompts, evidence text, salts, or payout accounts.
46. Transitive attribution reports: downstream providers publish signed reports
    that bind copied RDLLM output to the upstream capsule and response envelope,
    verify copied-body hashes, carry original source rows forward, and allocate a
    pass-through share of downstream creator-pool value to the upstream owners.
47. Clearinghouse reports: providers, creator collectives, marketplaces, or
    auditors normalize royalty statements and transitive reports into payable,
    escrow, and held rows so cross-provider settlement can reject duplicates and
    preserve an auditable trail of repeated or overlapping submissions.
48. Remittance reports: providers or creator collectives convert clearinghouse
    payable and escrow rows into instruction-only payment rows, payout-account
    hash bindings, reconciliation references, and preserved hold rows without
    exposing bank details or claiming that payment settlement already occurred.
49. Revenue allocation reports: providers convert hashed billing, advertising,
    subscription, API, enterprise, or marketplace revenue pools into per-event
    gross revenue before creator-pool payout, while proving source revenue
    conservation and withholding private customer or invoice text.
50. Finance ledger attestations: providers reconcile those revenue pools to
    hash-only external finance exports such as billing, invoice, ad-server,
    API-meter, enterprise-contract, or marketplace-order records while withholding
    customer, payment, prompt, output, source, and evidence text.
51. Proof dependency graphs: providers publish a cycle-checked, hash-only replay
    DAG that tells auditors and downstream providers which artifacts verify before
    which downstream artifacts, while separating hard replay dependencies from
    publication commitments.
52. Publication monitors: providers publish append-only public proof-surface
    checkpoints with Merkle snapshot roots, artifact diffs, and certification
    regression checks so attribution evidence can be monitored after release.
53. Publication witnesses: independent witnesses cosign publication-monitor
    checkpoints under quorum policy and split-view detection so providers cannot
    quietly present inconsistent source-proof or certification histories to
    different creators, customers, auditors, or users.
54. Trust registries: providers publish active and revoked provider, auditor, and
    witness key hashes, key IDs, rotations, artifact signature bindings, and
    witness signature bindings so public proof verification is not anchored in
    unverifiable provider key claims.

The current reference suite also adds generated-code attribution as a distinct
certification surface: code outputs are scored against registered snippets, checked
for license compatibility, paid or escrowed, and published only as hash and feature
commitments.

For event `r`, let `G_r` be gross monetizable revenue. In the upgraded mechanism
`G_r` is not merely provider-supplied input: it is recomputed from one or more
declared source-revenue pools. For source pool `j` with gross amount `R_j` and
event basis weight `w_r`, the allocation report computes:

```text
A_{r,j} = R_j * w_r / sum_k w_k
G_r = sum_j A_{r,j}
```

The verifier rejects the report unless source pools conserve into event allocations
and the allocated `G_r` matches the ledger event gross revenue. Let `s` be the
creator pool rate. The event-level pool is:

```text
P_r = G_r * s
```

For each eligible source chunk `i`, the prototype computes:

```text
raw_i = (
  0.15 * retrieval_i
  + 0.15 * output_support_i
  + 0.05 * prompt_overlap_i
  + 0.55 * text_match_i
  + 0.10 * citation_i
) * training_value_i
weight_i = raw_i / sum(raw_j)
payout_i = P_r * weight_i
```

Eligibility is policy-scoped and registry-scoped. A source can be allowed for
training or retrieval but prohibited for generation, display, quote, or external
attribution. If a generated output matches a source that is denied for the attempted
use, the event is marked `rights_blocked` and the creator pool is routed to
rights-conflict escrow rather than normal payout. If the matched work is under an
open duplicate-ownership conflict, the event is marked `registry_disputed` and the
creator pool is routed to registry-dispute escrow until the claim is resolved.

The prototype uses transparent channels for retrieval relevance, output support,
prompt overlap, text match strength, citation evidence, and training-value priors.
The `training_value_i` term allows production systems to incorporate training-data
value, quality review, license tier, or editorial importance.

## 4. Implementation

The accompanying prototype implements registry, ownership-conflict detection,
retrieval, text matching, training valuation, claim grounding, source-footer
rendering, attribution, settlement, escrow release, escrow, and audit layers in
Python with no external dependencies. It uses TF-IDF retrieval, n-gram text matching,
longest common token sequence matching, duplicate-content scoring, and Shapley-style
training priors as transparent stand-ins for production attribution signals. The
semantic attribution report adds deterministic concept normalization,
source-distinctiveness scores, decoy margins, public footer rows, accepted-owner
payouts, and semantic escrow for paraphrased or externally generated text. The
deterministic answer composer can be replaced by a production LLM if the same
retrieval and output data are passed into the attribution layer.

Each event records:

- prompt, raw answer, and rendered output
- gross revenue and creator pool
- retrieved chunks, text matches, and content hashes
- source-access records for retrieval and text matching
- rights policy decisions
- ownership registry decisions
- source references shown to the user
- claim support checks, evidence text, span hashes, character offsets, and grounding coverage
- grounding quality verdict and component scores
- attribution-gap verdict, classifications, scores, and report hash
- attribution basis scores
- contribution weights
- payouts
- event hash

The audit function checks payout conservation, weight normalization, source-hash
integrity, source-reference integrity, claim evidence span integrity,
grounding-quality reproducibility, rights-decision binding, registry-decision
binding, attribution-gap reproducibility, source-access reconciliation, and
event-hash reproducibility.

The ownership registry emits `rdllm-claim-registry/v1` reports. A report commits to
registered works, optional ownership attestations, duplicate or near-duplicate
conflicts, open-conflict counts, and a report hash. In enforcement mode, a matched
conflicted work is not paid directly: source quotes are withheld, the rendered
answer states that registry-dispute escrow was used, and the receipt binds the
conflict ID and registry report hash to the event.

After dispute resolution, the prototype emits an `rdllm-escrow-resolution/v1`
settlement report instead of rewriting the original event. The report binds the
resolution ID, conflict ID, registry report hash, evidence URI and hash, original
event hashes, recipient splits, balances before release, balances after release, a
report hash, and an optional signature. This gives creators economic finality while
preserving the historical fact that the response-time event was disputed.

The receipt layer commits to the event hash, prompt hash, answer hash, rendered-output
hash, grounding report, grounding-quality report, attribution-gap report,
source-access trace, source references, claim evidence spans, rights decisions,
registry decisions, payout shares, model route, issuer, and signature metadata. A
public receipt discloses source IDs, hashes, source URIs, grounding coverage, claim
support, evidence span hashes, contribution weights, and a selective-disclosure
root, while a private receipt can retain evidence quotes, exact evidence text,
source-access traces, detailed policy/registry decisions, and economic details. A
selective-disclosure package publishes path/value/salt/leaf-hash tuples for public
fields and path/hash commitments for redacted fields. Verifiers recompute the root
and reject public receipt drift or disclosed leaf tampering. A Merkle transparency
log provides inclusion proofs so third parties can verify that a receipt existed at
a given tree root without trusting the model provider's dashboard.

The trace-exchange layer emits `rdllm-trace-exchange/v1` artifacts aligned with
OpenTelemetry GenAI spans. Each trace contains one generation span, one source-access
span for every retrieval or text match, one citation span per footer-visible source,
and one claim-support span per checked claim. The signed receipt carries hashes over
source accesses, visible source references, and claim support; the trace verifier
rejects omitted provider-access spans, relabeled citation content hashes, claim
evidence drift, or summary hashes that no longer match the receipt.

The royalty-statement layer emits `rdllm-royalty-statement/v1` reports for periodic
creator accounting. A statement aggregates many events into creator totals, escrow
totals, work totals, and source-usage rows, while carrying commitments over event
rollups, royalty shares, source access, source references, claim support, receipts,
and traces. It intentionally excludes prompts, rendered outputs, answer text, source
quotes, evidence text, and matched text. A private auditor can recompute the
statement from the ledger and bound artifacts; tampering with creator totals,
source-usage counts, dropped traces, or payout conservation breaks verification.

The attribution-challenge layer emits `rdllm-attribution-challenge/v1` reports for
creator appeals. A report binds an original event hash, optional statement hash,
claimant work and chunk hash, text-overlap evidence, already-credited status, and a
non-rewrite remedy. Accepted omissions can produce a corrective claimant payment;
unlicensed but influential sources can route to rights-conflict escrow; weak claims
are rejected; and already credited sources receive no double payment.

The interoperability layer emits `rdllm-interop/v1` bundles. A bundle contains a
VC-shaped attribution credential, a PROV-shaped provenance graph for the answer, an
OpenTelemetry-aligned trace exchange, and optionally a VC-shaped escrow-settlement
credential. The graph represents prompt and
output commitments, source chunk entities, claim entities, creator/model/issuer
agents, generation activities, supported-claim relations, paid-share relations,
rights decisions, registry decisions, and attribution-gap decisions. This allows
provider APIs, creator
registries, enterprise governance systems, and transparency services to consume the
same attribution facts without depending on RDLLM's internal ledger format.

A conformance verifier checks the full bundle: ledger event, rendered source footer,
claim span hashes, private receipt, optional public receipt, signature, transparency
log, inclusion proof, trace exchange, royalty statement, challenge report, and
provider attribution card. The answer provenance card adds the user-facing
verification layer by binding the rendered footer and claim span hashes back to the
same receipt and trace. This moves the proposal closer to procurement-grade
infrastructure because customers and regulators can test artifacts independently
rather than trusting a provider's UI. The source verification report adds the next
layer: it proves that cited source hashes, quote hashes, source URIs, claim evidence
spans, footer span prefixes, and answer-card bindings materialize from the
registered corpus without publishing private source or evidence text.
The response envelope converts these proofs into an API-native object by packaging
the rendered answer with embedded public artifacts that a customer can verify
without private ledger access.
The integration profile adds the provider-facing contract: it publishes the
generation and verification endpoints, response-envelope format, required headers,
schema map, verifier commands, public surfaces, readiness checks, and bound hashes
that make an RDLLM-compatible API independently testable.
The discovery manifest turns those public artifacts into a single well-known
verification entry point. A provider can publish `/.well-known/rdllm.json` so
customers, creators, auditors, and downstream platforms can discover the provider
card, certification report, integration profile, response envelope, assurance
bundle, optional training summary, schemas, verifier commands, API contract hash,
and artifact hashes without relying on custom integration instructions.
The attribution exchange manifest converts that provider-local publication into a
cross-provider relay contract. A downstream model provider or answer engine can
import upstream provider-card, certification, integration-profile,
discovery-manifest, response-envelope, assurance-bundle, and semantic-text reports
by declared hash and payload hash, preserve public source-footer rows, and carry
escrow obligations forward without receiving private prompts, outputs, matched
text, or source text.
The conformance vector pack then turns the mechanism into a reproducible
implementation target: it publishes fixture hashes, expected public outcomes,
verifier commands, and negative mutations so a foundation-model provider can prove
compatibility by reproducing the same successes and failures.
The federation handshake converts that compatibility target into a runtime
interoperability gate. Before a downstream provider consumes an upstream answer,
proof pack, or generated work, it can require a signed
`rdllm-federation-handshake/v1` artifact binding the requested minimum level,
negotiated level, provider identity, requester identity, provider-card hash,
certification hash, integration-profile hash, discovery hash, response-envelope
hash, attribution-exchange hash, conformance-vector hash, runtime headers,
source-footer relay, escrow relay, and downgrade rejection. This prevents a
provider from publishing strong proofs offline while silently omitting them at the
API boundary.
The attribution capsule closes the portability gap after the API boundary. A
copied answer, reposted response, exported report, or generated-asset metadata can
carry a `rdllm-attribution-capsule/v1` marker that binds the rendered-output hash
to the response envelope, federation handshake, attribution exchange, conformance
vector pack, provider card, discovery manifest, runtime capsule headers, a
C2PA-compatible assertion pointer, and a SCITT-like statement subject. The capsule
also includes a delivered-output contract: a verifier removes the capsule marker
from copied text, canonicalizes the remaining body, and checks that its hash still
matches the signed rendered-output hash. The capsule does not embed the prompt,
answer text, matched text, source text, hidden states, or private ledger payloads;
it gives users and platforms a verification handle for the same source-footer and
royalty evidence after content has been copied, while rejecting copies whose footer
survived but whose answer body changed.
The transitive attribution report closes the second-hop laundering gap. If that
copied answer becomes input to a downstream model, publication system, agent, or
marketplace, a `rdllm-transitive-attribution-report/v1` binds the downstream usage
event to the upstream capsule and response envelope. It verifies the copied marker
and copied-body hash, imports the upstream answer-card source rows, and computes
pass-through settlement obligations over the downstream creator pool. Downstream
systems can add local sources and settle residual value locally, but they cannot
claim that the copied AI output is a fresh unattributed source.
The clearinghouse report turns those provider-local and second-hop obligations
into market settlement. A `rdllm-clearinghouse-report/v1` ingests public royalty
statements and transitive attribution reports, verifies declared hashes, normalizes
creator/work/chunk obligations, aggregates payable and escrow totals, and moves
duplicate artifact submissions, overlapping statement events, or repeated
transitive obligations into held rows rather than paying them twice. The report
does not disclose prompts, outputs, source text, copied answers, evidence text, or
payout-account details; it publishes roots over the input artifacts, normalized
obligations, payable rows, escrow rows, held rows, duplicate rows, and policy.
The remittance report closes the last-mile accounting gap without overclaiming
financial execution. A `rdllm-remittance-report/v1` takes a ready clearinghouse
report plus the creator license contract and emits instruction-only payment rows.
Each row binds the clearinghouse settlement row hash, origin hashes, chunk IDs,
creator license contract hash, payout-account hash, ISO 20022-compatible
end-to-end ID, and remittance reference. Escrow rows are routed to escrow-account
hashes, duplicate or overlap holds remain held, and the report explicitly states
that it is not evidence of completed bank settlement.
The payment execution report closes the finality gap. A
`rdllm-payment-execution-report/v1` takes the remittance report plus hash-only
external processor and escrow settlement records, then verifies that every
instruction's amount, currency, end-to-end ID, payout or escrow account hash,
processor status, processor-record hash, and settlement-batch hash match. It
rejects duplicate processor rows, unmatched rows, failed statuses, wrong-account
records, amount drift, currency drift, and raw bank or tax fields. Holds stay as
unpaid carryforward rows. This separates a provider's intent to pay from externally
attested execution and gives auditors a concrete boundary for when value can be
called paid, escrowed, or still outstanding.
The payment rail attestation closes the fabricated-processor-row gap. A
`rdllm-payment-rail-attestation/v1` takes the payment execution report, the trust
registry, and signed processor batch attestations, then verifies that every payment
or escrow processor is registered with an allowed role and that its signature binds
the execution report hash, settlement batch hash, record hashes, row hashes, totals,
currency, count, and settlement status. Public verifiers see hashes, signatures,
roles, and aggregate totals rather than bank, customer, tax, prompt, output, or
source text.
The creator payout receipt report closes the creator-facing finality gap. A
`rdllm-creator-payout-receipt-report/v1` takes the clearinghouse, remittance,
payment-execution, and payment-rail reports, then emits one replayable receipt row
per paid, escrowed, or held creator obligation. Each row binds the creator, work,
chunk IDs, origin hashes, clearinghouse settlement row, remittance instruction,
execution row, processor record hash, signed rail batch, and visible status. This
lets creators verify where an answer or generated artifact sourced value from and
whether that value moved, while public artifacts expose only hashes, IDs, statuses,
counts, currencies, and totals.
The third-party audit attestation closes the provider-trust gap. A provider can
publish many correct-looking artifacts while customers still need an independent
statement that the public proof pack was replayed. RDLLM therefore adds
`rdllm-third-party-audit-attestation/v1`: a hash-only external attestation over the
provider card, certification report, integration profile, discovery manifest,
assurance bundle, response envelope, source-confidence report, citation-footer
contract, clearinghouse report, remittance report, optional payment execution
report, optional creator payout receipt report, optional rendered attribution audit,
revenue allocation report, and
finance ledger attestation. It records
independent auditor identity, public verifier replay checks, negative controls, and
privacy-preserving commitments, then rejects stale hashes, missing audit discovery
surfaces, response drift, footer suppression, remittance amount drift, payment
execution drift, revenue allocation drift, finance ledger drift, provider
self-attestation, signature drift, and private text or payout-account leakage.
The revenue allocation report closes the gross-revenue trust gap. A platform can
have perfect source attribution and still underpay creators if event revenue is an
unverified number. RDLLM therefore adds `rdllm-revenue-allocation-report/v1`,
which binds hashed billing, advertising, subscription, API, enterprise, or
marketplace revenue sources to per-event allocation rows, receipt hashes, ledger
gross revenue, creator-pool rates, expected creator pools, and royalty-share roots.
The verifier rejects non-conserved source pools, event gross drift, creator-pool
drift, missing receipt bindings, duplicate event rows, private billing fields, and
signature drift.
The finance ledger attestation closes the finance-source trust gap. A conserved
allocation pool is still insufficient if the provider can invent the source pool
itself. RDLLM therefore adds `rdllm-finance-ledger-attestation/v1`, which binds
hash-only finance export rows to the revenue-source pools used by the allocation
report. Each row records a source ID, record type, external record hash, currency,
gross amount, event ID where available, allocation-policy reference, and row hash;
source rollups then prove finance totals and allocation totals match. The verifier
rejects missing external hashes, source IDs with no allocation pool, allocation
pools with no backing finance row, source-total drift, total gross drift, currency
drift, duplicate finance rows, stale hashes, signature drift, and leakage of
customer IDs, invoice text, payment details, prompts, outputs, source text, or
evidence text.
The proof dependency graph closes the replay-order trust gap. Once a provider
publishes dozens of artifacts, an auditor should not have to infer verifier order
from prose or accidentally validate an artifact before its prerequisites. RDLLM
therefore adds `rdllm-proof-dependency-graph/v1`: a hash-only DAG whose nodes are
public proof artifacts and whose edges distinguish hard replay dependencies from
publication commitments such as Merkle-bundle inclusion. The graph emits a
topological replay order and verifier steps; its verifier rejects cycles, unknown
dependencies, missing replay steps, stale graph hashes, non-reproducible artifact
hashes, publication edges that overclaim assurance-bundle inclusion,
private-field leakage, and signature drift.
The publication monitor closes the post-publication drift gap. A provider can pass
certification and later change the proof surface behind user-visible source
footers, so RDLLM adds `rdllm-publication-monitor/v1`: a signed checkpoint history
over provider cards, certification reports, integration profiles, response
envelopes, assurance bundles, proof dependency graphs, and optional public
artifacts. Each checkpoint binds artifact hashes into a Merkle snapshot, links to
the previous checkpoint, reports added/removed/changed artifacts, and rejects
required-artifact removal, non-reproducible hashes, snapshot-proof failures,
certification-level regression, private-field leakage, and checkpoint-chain
tampering. The result is not merely a payout ledger, but a monitorable public
attribution surface that can keep answer footers grounded after release.
The publication witness closes the split-view gap left by any single monitor. A
provider could otherwise try to show one checkpoint history to a creator and a
different checkpoint history to an enterprise customer. RDLLM adds
`rdllm-publication-witness/v1`: a signed report that extracts checkpoint subjects
from publication monitors, asks independent witnesses to attest to those subjects,
enforces a quorum, detects equivocation by issuer and checkpoint index, and
publishes only hashes and metadata. The verifier recomputes monitor subjects,
witness signatures, attestation hashes, quorum status, split-view status, report
hash, and provider signature.
The trust registry closes the signer-trust gap left by publication witnesses.
RDLLM adds `rdllm-trust-registry/v1`: a public registry that binds active provider,
auditor, and witness principals to key hashes, key IDs, allowed algorithms,
artifact signature bindings, witness-attestation bindings, rotations, and revoked
keys without exposing raw key material. The verifier recomputes artifact
signatures, witness signatures, key hashes, rotation links, revocation status,
registry hash, and provider signature before a client accepts a public footer or
royalty proof.
The certification attestation closes the certification-trust gap left by a bare
report hash. RDLLM adds `rdllm-certification-attestation/v1`: a signed object that
binds the certification report hash, attested level, levels root, case-status
root, certifier identity, target provider, validity metadata, and privacy flags.
The verifier recomputes the report hash, roots, attestation hash, and signature,
and the trust registry can bind the certifier key to that attestation without
embedding private certification case payloads.
The response release gate closes the final serving gap. Recent citation-verification
work shows that a plausible citation or working link does not prove answer support;
therefore RDLLM adds `rdllm-response-release-gate/v1` as an emit-time decision.
The gate recomputes the response envelope, answer provenance card, source
verification report, attribution capsule, provider card, and certification report,
then returns `emit` only when footer source labels, claim span prefixes, source
materialization, capsule delivery contract, public provider surfaces, and minimum
`RDLLM-L34` upstream certification all verify. Unsupported or unverifiable answers
are held before display rather than released with decorative source footers.
The proof-carrying response makes that decision enforceable in the delivered API
object. A `rdllm-proof-carrying-response/v1` object embeds the displayed payload,
response envelope, attribution capsule, release gate, provider card, and
certification report. If the gate verifies, the object releases the answer plus the
capsule marker; if the gate fails, the object returns a held-response notice and
suppresses the original answer. This is the point where attribution stops being
metadata about a response and becomes a proof-carrying response format.
The serving gateway report closes the operational egress gap. A provider can
publish strong proofs yet still route some production answers around them unless
the serving boundary itself is auditable. RDLLM therefore adds
`rdllm-serving-gateway-report/v1`: a signed API-route report that hash-commits the
private prompt and raw model output, embeds the proof-carrying response, binds the
release-gate and proof-response hashes, and records the delivered-output hash. Its
verifier rejects egress drift when the delivered output is not exactly the
proof-carrying response copied output.
The creator license contract closes the pre-use rights gap. Attribution footers
tell a user which works supported an answer, but they do not prove that those
works were licensed, compensable, or contractually bound before use. RDLLM
therefore adds `rdllm-creator-license-contract/v1`: a signed rights artifact that
hash-commits every registered work, maps it to allowed and prohibited uses,
requires attribution and compensation duties, records ODRL/Croissant/SPDX-style
policy metadata, and redacts raw work text and payout account details. Its
verifier rejects missing duties, license-scope drift, content-hash mismatches,
term-hash drift, signature drift, and private text leakage.
The source confidence report closes the user-trust gap. A footer can list real
sources and still fail the user if the source is stale, weakly supportive,
unlicensed for generation, or not actually connected to a claim. RDLLM therefore
adds `rdllm-source-confidence-report/v1`: a signed public artifact that joins the
answer provenance card, source verification report, and creator license contract
into verified/warning/failed footer rows, claim confidence rows, and a
hallucination taxonomy for fabricated sources, metadata drift, hash mismatches,
footer omissions, attribution suppression, license gaps, unsupported claims, and
evidence-span gaps. The artifact discloses source metadata, hash prefixes, scores,
and row hashes while redacting prompt text, answer text, source text, claim text,
evidence text, and payout accounts.
The citation footer contract closes the final display gap. A verified footer can
still be weakened by a downstream client that omits a row, changes display order,
strips confidence status, or hides license and royalty duties. RDLLM therefore
adds `rdllm-citation-footer-contract/v1`: a signed contract that turns the
source-confidence rows into exact source lines and claim anchors. It binds display
order, source labels, titles, creators, work IDs, source URIs, content-hash
prefixes, confidence labels, license status, royalty status, evidence-span
prefixes, row hashes, footer hash, and response-boundary hash. The verifier
recomputes the contract from the response envelope and rejects display drift,
inactive license or royalty duties, failed confidence rows, signature drift, and
private-text leakage. This makes the footer both user-readable and machine-
enforceable.
The rendered attribution audit closes the visible-answer gap. A valid footer
contract can still be undermined if the final Markdown body invents a source
marker, strips a footer row, appends an unsupported claim after the footer, or
changes claim-evidence span anchors. RDLLM therefore adds
`rdllm-rendered-attribution-audit/v1`: a signed audit that parses the exact
displayed answer body, `Sources` footer, and `Claim Evidence` rows. It verifies
body `[S#]` markers against the response envelope, footer rows against the
citation footer contract, and claim-evidence spans against source availability,
evidence sufficiency, counterevidence, and answer-coverage reports. The audit
stores line hashes and source metadata rather than raw prompt, answer, source,
claim, or evidence text, turning visible citations into replayable evidence rather
than a decorative confidence signal.
The training-memory provenance audit closes the parametric-memory gap. A response
can reproduce registered training text without any fresh retrieval event, making
access logs and visible footers look clean while the answer still carries
owner-controlled expression. RDLLM therefore adds
`rdllm-training-memory-provenance/v1`: a signed, hash-only report that scans the
exact displayed answer against registered source snapshots, binds detected spans
to training-content summaries and creator-license contracts, and requires visible
footer attribution before display. Hidden memorized spans create remediation rows
or escrow instead of being silently released.
The evidence-locked generation audit closes the post-hoc citation gap. A response
can be generated first and cited afterward unless the serving path proves that
each support-bearing answer unit was bound to evidence before emission. RDLLM
therefore adds `rdllm-evidence-locked-generation/v1`: a signed, hash-only report
that binds answer-unit hashes, claim hashes, source labels, generation-context
blocks, source-access IDs, footer rows, body markers, and rendered claim-evidence
spans to a lock created before generation starts. Late locks, unbound footer rows,
hidden memorized spans, and raw answer/source/context leakage fail verification.
The emission evidence enforcement audit closes the serving-boundary gap. A valid
evidence lock is insufficient if the API or streaming layer can emit a different
payload. RDLLM therefore adds `rdllm-emission-evidence-enforcement/v1`: a signed,
hash-only report that binds proof-carrying response delivery, serving-gateway
egress, streaming chunk commitments, and L82 evidence-lock rows to the same
rendered and copied output hashes. Streaming before the lock window, output-hash
drift, non-replayable chunk chains, unlocked support-bearing units, and raw
answer/source/context/chunk leakage fail verification.
The live emission witness audit closes the self-attested-serving gap. A valid
emission enforcement report still originates inside the provider boundary unless
independent observers can attest to the preflight and completion states of the
stream. RDLLM therefore adds `rdllm-live-emission-witness/v1`: a signed,
hash-only witness report over the L83 emission report and streaming manifest.
Accepted reports require independent preflight quorum before the first chunk,
completion quorum over the final stream chain after the last chunk, organization
diversity, reproducible witness signatures, and matching chunk-subject roots.
Late preflight, stream drift, witness disagreement, and raw
answer/source/context/chunk leakage fail verification.
The live emission transparency audit closes the witness-withholding and
equivocation gap. A provider can satisfy independent live witnessing while still
showing different witness histories to different auditors unless witness subjects
are externally logged. RDLLM therefore adds
`rdllm-live-emission-transparency/v1`: a signed hash-only report requiring the
L84 live witness report and every preflight/completion witness attestation to
appear in append-only transparency logs with valid inclusion proofs, reproducible
Merkle roots, append-only prefix consistency, matching subject payload hashes,
matching entry types, and no same-size split-view roots. This follows the
transparency-service and witness pattern used by SCITT, Sigstore/Rekor, and
transparency-dev witnesses, but applies it directly to emission-time attribution
proofs [@ietf2026scitt; @sigstoreRekor; @transparencyDevWitness].
The attested attribution runtime closes the measured-enforcement gap. Logged
witness subjects prove what observers saw, but not which runtime enforced the
evidence lock, release gate, footer contract, and gateway binding. RDLLM therefore
adds `rdllm-attested-attribution-runtime/v1`: a hash-only report binding the L85
transparency report, proof-carrying response, serving-gateway report, and
evidence-locked-generation report to a runtime measurement, subject binding root,
fresh nonce, trusted attestor quote, and required attribution capabilities. The
reference implementation uses HMAC conformance quotes so the mechanism is runnable;
production deployments can substitute hardware TEE, confidential-computing, ZKML,
or hybrid attestation evidence while preserving the same verification surface. This
follows the RATS architecture distinction between attesters, verifiers, relying
parties, evidence, and attestation results, and applies confidential-computing
attestation to attribution enforcement rather than only data-in-use secrecy
[@ietf2023rats; @confidentialComputingConsortium].
The claim-source attribution audit closes the "grounded answer but ungrounded
footer" gap. DataDignity frames robust provenance as a pinpoint ranking problem
that must distinguish answer-critical support from topical or lexical resemblance,
including hard anti-documents [@li2026datadignity]. Source-attribution work for
RAG treats attribution as a per-document contribution problem rather than a
retrieval-list display problem [@nematov2025sourceAttributionRag], while GaRAGe
and Trust-Align show that evaluation must include claim grounding, citation
attribution, and refusal when support is insufficient
[@sorodoc2025garage; @song2025trustalign]. Chain of Evidence shows that visual
document RAG needs page and bounding-box evidence rather than text-only links
[@liu2026chainEvidence], and GuarantRAG separates parametric answers from
evidence-backed refer answers to reduce internal-knowledge conflicts
[@zhao2026guarantrag]. RDLLM therefore adds
`rdllm-claim-source-attribution/v1`: a hash-only report that replays each visible
claim against candidate sources, Q&A nugget commitments, anti-documents, optional
visual-region commitments, LOO-style source contribution, footer rows, and payout
rows before a source can be credited.
Recent retrieval-utility work argues that answer-only or citation-only evaluation
misses whether an evidence item operationally changed the generated answer
[@jain2026cuer], while agentic retrieval work shows that production systems now
use multi-step search, open, find, and summarize traces rather than a single static
retrieval list [@suresh2026agenticrag; @wang2026retrievalReasoning]. Multimodal
fact-level attribution further shows that correct reasoning can still attach
hallucinated modality or time citations [@wan2026murgat], and the survey literature
identifies fragmented evidence granularity and evaluation metrics as an open
standardization gap [@schreieder2025attributionCitationQuotation]. RDLLM therefore
adds `rdllm-causal-evidence-utility/v1`: a hash-only report that binds the
claim-source footer to current-turn retrieval/tool traces and REMOVE, REPLACE,
DUPLICATE, and multi-source removal trials. A credited source must show causal
utility, duplicates must not inflate credit, synergistic multi-hop evidence is
credited jointly, and prior-context citation drift routes the affected row away
from footer credit and royalty payment.
Parametric-memory attribution closes the remaining foundation-model gap: claims
that are answered from learned weights rather than current-turn retrieval. Recent
knowledge-attribution probes distinguish memory-driven answers from context-driven
answers using hidden representations [@brink2026probing],
probabilistic attribution provides model-agnostic token sensitivity from
next-token probabilities [@shilpika2026probattr], and ProToken
shows token-level responsibility can be localized in privacy-preserving federated
LLM settings [@gill2026protoken]. Protocol-layer proposals such as SCP argue that
creator-owned content needs default attribution at the data-access layer rather
than only after litigation or disclosure [@panchigar2026scp]. RDLLM therefore adds
`rdllm-parametric-memory-attribution/v1`: a hash-only report that binds accepted
sources to training-content summaries, memory-recall probes, influence or
model-signal evidence, anti-document separation, current-context contamination
checks, source footers, and creator-pool conservation.
Style-influence attribution closes the adjacent creative-imitation gap: outputs
that do not copy protected text but still imitate a registered writing style,
voice, or modality-specific creator signature. RDLLM adds
`rdllm-style-influence-attribution/v1`, a hash-only report that requires active
style-generation permission, sufficient style or media-signature similarity,
declared style intent, separation from anti-style decoys, low content-copy
overlap, footer disclosure, and creator-pool conservation before style credit or
royalties are released. Copy-like outputs route back to semantic/copy
attribution, and unlicensed or ambiguous style claims route to escrow.
Model-lineage attribution closes the downstream training and distillation gap:
when an attributed output, synthetic dataset row, or teacher trace is used to
train, fine-tune, or distill a student model, the original source obligations
must survive into future usage. This follows recent post-training data-lineage
work showing that dataset evolution, synthetic aggregation, and benchmark
contamination propagate along lineage paths [@zhang2026tracingRoots], model
provenance work requiring coverage guarantees rather than heuristic single-source
claims [@qiu2026mps], and post-training analysis showing that data composition can
embed downstream behavior changes in model weights [@karouzos2026diversityCollapse].
RDLLM adds
`rdllm-model-lineage-attribution/v1`, a hash-only report that binds the student
model, training run, training-content summary, synthetic disclosure, teacher
distribution or teacher-output evidence, inherited source rows, duplicate
artifact guards, rights escrow, future usage pool, footer rows, and
creator-pool conservation. Hidden synthetic training and missing teacher evidence

Black-box model provenance closes the dishonest-disclosure gap: if a downstream
provider does not publish model-lineage evidence, an auditor can still challenge
the API-visible model with prompts, baseline response distributions, behavior
fingerprints, refusal-vector or watermark evidence, and multiple-testing
controls. This follows black-box provenance testing for derived models
[@nikolic2025modelProvenanceTesting], provable provenance sets [@qiu2026mps],
localized watermarks whose signal can transfer through distillation
[@sander2026textseal], privacy-preserving verifiable watermark detection
[@luan2026vow], refusal-vector behavioral fingerprints
[@xu2026behavioralFingerprint], and text-preserving fine-tune provenance audits
[@li2025textPreservingAudit]. RDLLM adds
`rdllm-black-box-model-provenance/v1`, a hash-only challenge report that
publishes the challenged model commitment, challenge hashes, candidate provenance
set, empirical baseline p-values, Bonferroni-adjusted confidence checks,
watermark or fingerprint support counts, footer rows, and unresolved derivative
settlement or escrow obligations without exposing private prompts, source text,
or model outputs.
Attribution dispute adjudication closes the enforcement gap after a source,
model, or payout claim is contested. The earlier layers can show that a source was
used, that a downstream model is likely derivative, and that a creator pool is
owed; they do not by themselves specify how disputed value is frozen, how rebuttal
evidence is admitted, how conflicted verifiers are excluded, or when escrow can be
released. RDLLM adds
`rdllm-attribution-dispute-adjudication-report/v1`, a hash-only report that binds
the subject proof hash, claimant rows, respondent rebuttal rows, evidence
commitments, bonded verifier votes, conflict disclosures, appeal state, release
rows, freeze rows, slash rows, bounty rows, and adjudicated footer rows. This
follows the same transparency and signed-statement architecture used by content
credentials, SCITT statements, append-only witnesses, and verifiable credentials
[@c2pa2026spec; @ietf2026scitt; @transparencyDevWitness], while reusing recent
model-provenance and watermark/fingerprint evidence as admissible inputs
[@nikolic2025modelProvenanceTesting; @sander2026textseal; @luan2026vow;
@xu2026behavioralFingerprint]. Verification rejects missing notice, insufficient
evidence commitments, failed quorum, conflicted quorum votes, premature release
during appeal, unconserved escrow, settlement rows that do not match the decision,
and private prompt, source, output, or challenge transcript leakage.
Post-adjudication settlement adjustment closes the accounting gap after a dispute
changes attribution but payment execution, escrow release, or creator payout
receipts already exist. YouTube's Content ID dispute process demonstrates the
operational pattern of separately holding disputed revenue and paying it to the
proper party after resolution [@youtubeContentIDDisputedRevenue], but AI systems
need the same principle generalized across model APIs, clearinghouses, payment
processors, and future royalties. RDLLM adds
`rdllm-post-adjudication-settlement-adjustment-report/v1`, a forward-only
correction report that binds the L93 adjudication hash, original payment execution
or payout-receipt hashes, corrected entitlement rows, creator delta rows, top-up
rows, capped recoupment rows, future-netting rows, appeal freeze rows, and
creator-visible adjustment receipts. The report never mutates the historical
payment rows; it appends a new signed receipt that creators and auditors can
verify against the earlier settlement. Verification rejects missing processor or
receipt hashes, historical payment rewrites, uncapped recoupment, top-ups not
covered by escrow, appeal-time release, unconserved adjustment totals, missing
creator adjustment receipts, and private payment or source-evidence leakage.
The private audit challenge closes the confidential-evidence gap. Citation
verification papers show that visible references can fail at factual support even
when links and metadata look plausible [@onweller2026cited; @khajavi2026citecheck],
while PaperTrail shows that useful provenance often requires claim-evidence
inspection rather than document-level citations [@martinboyle2026papertrail].
RDLLM therefore adds `rdllm-private-audit-challenge/v1`: an auditor-nonce-bound
certificate over selected hidden receipt paths. A provider publishes only the path,
category, disclosure status, leaf hash, value hash, salt hash, opening commitment,
and row hash. An authorized verifier holding the private receipt recomputes those
openings against the selective-disclosure package and rejects replayed nonces,
missing source-access paths, mismatched claim-evidence commitments, rights-decision
drift, payout drift, and any leakage of private prompts, evidence text, source text,
salts, or payout accounts.
The derivative lineage report closes a different adoption gap: it binds source
royalty shares to upstream work edges so a summary, synthetic training example,
tool output, or transformed corpus can pay its immediate owner while passing a
configurable share through to original upstream owners. The report is independent
of the private work text; it uses work IDs, content hashes, lineage edges, payout
obligations, and payout-conservation commitments.
The provenance evaluation report closes the quality-evidence gap: it replays a
portable benchmark across exact source reuse, paraphrased source reuse, overlapping
decoys, unattributed escrow, and derivative-lineage cases. This gives model buyers
an auditable answer to whether the source finder works before they trust the
resulting royalty ledger.
The counterfactual influence report closes the source-presence/source-impact gap:
for each credited work, it removes the work, replays the same prompt and answer,
and records whether source credit disappears, payout reallocates or escrows, and
the credited source had decisive marginal influence. This turns citation into a
testable intervention claim rather than a decorative footer.
The media attribution report closes the modality gap: for each submitted image,
audio, video, 3D, or text-shaped media signature, it computes exact-hash,
perceptual-similarity, and descriptor-similarity evidence against registered
assets, publishes only commitments and scores, pays matched owners, and routes weak
or unknown matches to unattributed-media escrow.

The model signal attribution report closes the provider-internal gap. A provider
can submit private telemetry for log-probability deltas, activation similarity,
gradient influence, attention mass, and memorization score. The public report
contains an explicit attribution contract, scalar scores, source IDs, commitments,
payout shares, and model-signal escrow, while redacting hidden states, token logits,
private prompts, private outputs, and chain-of-thought. This separates the user's
visible citation surface from the model's private reliance evidence.

The rights remediation report closes the lifecycle-consent gap. A provider can
compare previous and updated rights states after opt-out, revocation, license
change, use restriction, jurisdiction change, or minimum-royalty update. The public
report preserves historical event hashes, publishes changed-policy commitments,
runs future-use probes, and verifies rights-conflict escrow for newly denied text
use while redacting work text, prompts, outputs, matched text, and private ledger
payloads.

The L53 layer adds generated-code attribution: generated code is hash-committed,
scored against registered source snippets, checked for license compatibility, paid
when compatible, and escrowed when copied-code signals conflict with the intended
output license or source policy.
The L54 layer adds pre-settlement claim verification: direct payout requires trusted
signed ownership evidence, while weak, duplicate, or unverified registrations route
to escrow/review before clearing or remittance.
The L55 layer adds source availability verification: citation footer rows must
resolve to reachable or archived source snapshots whose content hashes and cited
claim spans match the registered material, while the public report redacts source
bodies.
The L56 layer adds evidence sufficiency verification: every claim's cited span must
be the top-ranked minimal sufficient support, must clear a decoy-margin threshold,
and must remain bound to source availability, source materialization, and the
rendered footer before the answer can be treated as grounded.
The L57 layer adds counterevidence adjudication: every claim is checked against
registered source spans for contradiction candidates, and unaddressed
counterevidence blocks release even when the claim has a strong supporting span.
The L58 layer adds release grounding closure: when a provider claims L55-L57,
the public response envelope must embed and bind the source-availability,
evidence-sufficiency, and counterevidence reports, and the release gate must
verify that chain before answer emission.
The L59 layer adds answer claim coverage: every support-bearing sentence in the
public answer surface must replay to a verified, sufficient, counterevidence-free
claim row. This closes the final serving gap where a provider could append an
unsupported factual sentence to an otherwise grounded answer, including after the
citation footer.
The L60 layer adds generation context closure: every verified displayed claim must
replay to source-access spans and redacted generation-context block commitments
captured before delivery. This closes the post-hoc attribution gap where a
provider could answer first and attach plausible citations afterward.
The L61 layer adds source boundary integrity: every generation-context source
packet must be role-labeled as evidence, hash-bound to source-access telemetry,
excluded from control and instruction channels, and unable to modify attribution
or payout policy. This closes the prompt-injection boundary gap where retrieved
text could both supply evidence and attempt to steer the model or the royalty
mechanism.
The L62 layer adds decision provenance: claim grounding, visible footer rows,
royalty participation, and release decisions are represented as a hash-only
influence graph bound to the response envelope, release gate, trace exchange,
attribution capsule, and source-boundary report. This layer follows recent
provenance-aware agent security work such as ARGUS and AuthGraph
[@weng2026argus; @authgraph2026], but applies the graph boundary to attribution:
source text can justify evidence, while policy and payout influence must come from
explicit license, accounting, boundary, and release-gate proof channels.
The L63 layer adds calibrated attribution confidence: each public claim row,
source-footer row, and payout-participation row is tied to provenance-benchmark
case counts, observed rates, and lower confidence bounds. This layer follows
recent confidence and faithfulness work in RAG and LLM evaluation
[@geissler2026dependableRag; @gurArieh2026faithfulnessMetrics; @murugaraj2026ragvue]:
RDLLM should not imply that a cited source was certainly used unless the public
benchmark evidence supports that level of confidence. Low-confidence attribution is
therefore disclosed or routed to escrow, and verifiers reject benchmark drift,
missing decision provenance, private text leakage, and unreproducible confidence
bounds.
The L64 layer adds source authenticity and poisoning resilience: each public
source row must replay against source availability, source boundary integrity,
creator license terms, source-confidence rows, and trusted origin signals. Direct
payout requires origin signatures from trusted issuers, archive consensus, human
non-synthetic provenance for direct payment, low source-farm and poisoning risk,
reputable domains, content stability, and no citation-gaming signals. Rows that
fail these checks are warning-only and routed to escrow until origin review.

The L65 layer adds streaming attribution commitments: each streamed text chunk is
represented by public length, chunk hash, previous-chain hash, and chain hash rows
that replay from the proof-carrying response and serving gateway report. This
closes the streaming API gap where production model APIs emit incremental chunks
over event streams before a final object exists [@openaiStreamingResponses].
Verification reconstructs the stream from the public copied output and the
chunk-length commitments, then rejects reordered chunks, truncated streams,
rewritten chunks, missing proof-before-stream timing, or a final stream that omits
the attribution footer.

The L66 layer adds conversation attribution continuity: stateful follow-up turns
must carry inherited source-obligation hashes when they depend on prior turns or
previous-response IDs [@openai2026conversationState]. The L67 layer adds
agent-tool attribution trajectories: tool observations from hosted tools, custom
functions, files, web search, MCP servers, or remote systems must bind to trace
spans, claim rows, visible footer rows, and continuing source obligations
[@openai2026tools; @openai2026agentsTracing]. This follows trajectory-level agent
evaluation and evidence-grounding work [@trace2025toolAgents; @envtrustbench2026]
and closes the citation-audit gap observed in deep-research agents
[@onweller2026cited].

The L68 layer adds pinpoint provenance and anti-document guards. A source cannot
appear in a footer or receive direct payout merely because it is topically similar
to the answer. The report must show that candidate documents contain the
answer-critical facts, reject topical anti-documents, and route uncertain claims to
escrow. This operationalizes DataDignity's framing of attribution as pinpoint
provenance for LLM outputs [@li2026datadignity].

The L69 layer adds citation identity and metadata-swap guards. A footer source
cannot rely on a fake identifier, a real DOI attached to the wrong title, or a real
paper that does not support the bound claim. The report resolves declared citation
identifiers against authority records, checks title, author, year, identifier, and
claim-support consistency, emits canonical footer rows only for accepted citations,
and routes fabricated, unresolved, metadata-swapped, or claim-unsupported citations
to escrow. This responds to citation-audit results showing that link validity and
source topicality are not enough without claim-level verification
[@onweller2026cited; @khajavi2026citecheck].

The L70 layer adds attribution consensus. Direct settlement is allowed only when
source confidence, source authenticity, evidence sufficiency, counterevidence
adjudication, pinpoint provenance, and citation identity bind to the same event and
agree on the source row. Missing or conflicting channels route value to
`attribution_consensus_escrow` rather than averaging uncertainty into a payout. This
turns recent multi-source verification work, including DAVinCI's internal/external
attribution framing, into a settlement-grade quorum rule [@rawte2026davinci].

The L71 layer adds an independent verifier quorum. The provider can no longer be
the only party asserting that its L70 report is replayable: multiple external
verifiers must sign the same artifact root covering the attribution consensus
report, provider card, certification report, and integration profile. If the
accepted-verifier quorum, independent-organization quorum, signature checks, or
no-disagreement condition fail, every consensus payout row routes to
`verifier_quorum_escrow`. This converts the recent citation-verification lesson
from Cited but Not Verified and CiteCheck into an economic control: citations and
attribution rows must be externally replayable before money moves
[@onweller2026cited; @khajavi2026citecheck].

The L72 layer adds bonded verifier accountability. External signatures are useful,
but a production settlement standard also needs consequences for bad verifier
behavior. RDLLM therefore binds accepted L71 verifier rows to active
trust-registry identities, non-revoked verifier key hashes, slashable bond rows,
conflict disclosure hashes, challenge rows, and slashing-evidence commitments.
Direct settlement is released only when accepted verifiers are registered, bonded,
slashable, unchallenged, and conflict-clear; otherwise the same payout value routes
to `bonded_verifier_accountability_escrow`. This turns source grounding from a
provider promise into a verifier market with replayable accountability.

The L73 layer adds receipt transparency consistency. Even a signed payout report
can be gamed if the provider shows one receipt-log root to creators, another to
customers, and a third to witnesses. RDLLM therefore compares transparency-log
snapshots, recomputes tree sizes and Merkle roots, verifies append-only prefixes,
checks required receipt inclusion proofs, and rejects same-size split-view roots.
Direct settlement is released only when the required usage receipts appear in the
same consistent economic history; otherwise verifier-approved value routes to
`receipt_transparency_consistency_escrow`. This adapts Certificate
Transparency-style append-only consistency to AI usage royalties
[@ietf2021ct; @ietf2026scitt].

The L74 layer adds watchtower challenge settlement. Receipt transparency is
necessary but passive: a provider can still delay remediation unless independent
monitors have a public way to challenge the proof surface. RDLLM therefore binds
registered independent watchtowers to the L73 receipt-transparency subject,
requires a quorum of watchtower attestations, treats open or accepted public
challenges as settlement blockers, and emits hash-only slashing and bounty rows
when a challenge is accepted. Clean events can release through
`watchtower_challenge_settlement_ready`; challenged events route to
`watchtower_challenge_escrow`. This combines Certificate Transparency monitoring,
SCITT-style statement receipts, transparency witnesses, and optimistic
challenge/fraud-proof settlement patterns [@ietf2021ct; @ietf2026scitt;
@transparencyDevWitness; @picco2025dynamicFraudProof].

The L75 layer adds output provenance binding. Proof-carrying API responses are not
enough if attribution disappears when a user copies text, exports a file, posts a
snippet, or moves generated media into another platform. RDLLM therefore binds the
copied-output hash to the proof-carrying response, serving-gateway report,
attribution capsule, L74 watchtower-cleared settlement report, content-credential
assertion, watermark commitment, fingerprint-registry commitment, and public
verification path. The public artifact discloses hashes, credential rows, and
signal rows, but not raw generated text, prompts, source text, or evidence text.
This extends C2PA-style content credentials and durable watermarking into the
royalty proof surface: provenance metadata and copy-detection signals become
inputs to source attribution and settlement verification rather than stand-alone
authenticity labels [@c2paConformance; @openai2026provenance].

The L76 layer adds post-release discovery. Output-specific proof artifacts cannot
all exist in the initial well-known discovery manifest, because proof-carrying
responses, serving-gateway reports, attribution capsules, output-binding reports,
and replay graphs are created only after a particular answer is released. RDLLM
therefore publishes a second signed report that binds the base discovery manifest
to the late artifacts, but excludes its own hash from the artifact catalog. This
preserves a stable discovery endpoint while avoiding self-referential hash cycles.
Verifiers reject stale base manifests, missing late artifacts, output-binding
replay failure, catalog drift, and private text leakage. This turns provenance
discovery into a two-phase publication protocol: provider capability is advertised
before release, while output-specific attribution proof becomes discoverable after
release.

The L97 layer adds evidence-region binding. A system can otherwise cite the right
document while pointing users to the wrong passage, page, table, frame, or time
segment. RDLLM therefore binds every rendered claim span and footer span prefix to
an exact source snapshot region: page and line for text documents, character range
for transcripts and HTML, bounding box for visual/PDF evidence, and timecode for
audio or video evidence. The public report carries source-region hashes, actionable
locators, claim-region links, and hard wrong-region controls, but not raw prompt,
answer, source, customer, payment, or private evidence text. This extends recent
source-attribution work from link validity and topical relevance to region-level
factual support [@onweller2026cited; @shi2026citeaudit; @schreieder2025attributionCitationQuotation].

The L98 layer adds source access leases. A system can otherwise prove after the
fact that it cited, grounded, and paid a source while failing to prove that the
creator authorized the access event that fed retrieval, training, or generation.
RDLLM therefore binds every directly settled consumed source row to a
creator-issued lease signature, matching hash-only access log, active
creator-license term, minimum creator-pool-rate compliance, and exact evidence
region. Denied, expired, revoked, or unleased usage can be recorded, but it must
route to source-access escrow. This implements the runtime-access accountability
direction argued for by the Sovereign Context Protocol while preserving the
public-report privacy boundary [@panchigar2026scp].

The L99 layer adds content-protocol ingestion. This addresses a deployment gap
between publisher-facing protocols and provider-internal royalty ledgers: a model
provider can observe RSL, CoMP, SCP, ODRL, Croissant, robots.txt, or C2PA/TDM
signals, but lose or weaken those terms when converting them into internal
contracts and source-access leases. RDLLM therefore emits
`rdllm-content-protocol-ingestion/v1`, a hash-only report that records discovered
protocol rows, allowed and prohibited AI uses, payment type, attribution duty,
royalty duty, and minimum creator-pool rate, then proves the same terms were
preserved into creator-license contracts, source-access leases, and escrow
routing. External protocol denial, missing protocol records, or rate drift block
direct settlement [@rslStandard].

The L100 layer adds citation reliance receipts. Recent RAG attribution work shows
that a citation can be correct, or even support the statement, without proving
that the generation actually relied on that source [@wallat2025faithfulness;
@shi2026citeaudit]. RDLLM therefore emits
`rdllm-citation-reliance-receipt/v1`, a hash-only report that binds each
user-visible footer source to a pre-generation evidence lock, rendered
claim-evidence row, claim-source replay decision, causal evidence-utility row,
current-turn trace membership, source-access lease coverage, and content-protocol
permission. A source that is paid but hidden, visible but not locked before
generation, visible but not causal, or unlicensed remains in review or escrow
instead of being treated as faithful grounding.

The L101 layer adds license transaction receipts. Static rights declarations can
state what a publisher permits, but they do not prove that a license server
authorized the exact source access that later received direct settlement. RDLLM
therefore emits `rdllm-license-transaction-receipt/v1`, a hash-only report that
binds each directly settled source to a signed authorization token, a
license-ledger inclusion proof, the exact source-access nonce and meter hash, the
selected content-protocol record, and a valid issued/accepted/expiry window.
Missing, expired, unsigned, non-ledgered, access-mismatched, or protocol-drifted
transactions route the source to review or escrow instead of direct payment
[@rslStandard; @ietf2026scitt].

The L102 layer adds grounded source footer receipts. Recent citation-verification,
attribution-contract, and RAG faithfulness work shows that users need more than a
source-looking footer: each visible source row must replay to source identity,
factual support, actual reliance, confidence, availability, evidence-region
location, and license authorization. RDLLM therefore emits
`rdllm-grounded-source-footer/v1`, a privacy-preserving receipt that binds the
exact footer shown to the user to source-confidence rows, source-availability
rows, public evidence-region bindings, citation-reliance receipts,
license-transaction receipts, and public verifier handles, while redacting
prompt, answer, claim text, source text, evidence text, and payout data
[@onweller2026cited; @shi2026citeaudit; @wallat2025faithfulness;
@nguyen2026attributionContract].

The L103 layer adds source footer delivery receipts. This closes the gap between
an audit artifact and what a user actually receives: a provider can no longer
claim a grounded footer if the proof-carrying response is blocked, the copied
output differs from the gateway egress hash, source labels disappear, claim span
handles are omitted, or verifier metadata is stripped by a client or gateway.
`rdllm-source-footer-delivery/v1` binds the grounded footer hash to the response
envelope, proof-carrying response, serving-gateway report, delivered output hash,
source delivery rows, claim delivery rows, and public verifier commands. It is a
post-egress artifact, which avoids a hash cycle with the bootstrap discovery
manifest while still giving clients a replayable receipt before rendering or
relaying the answer [@ietf2026scitt; @shi2026citeaudit; @wallat2025faithfulness].

The L104 layer adds foundation API attribution profiles. This is the adoption
boundary for foundation-model APIs: generic clients need a compact response
contract, not bespoke knowledge of every RDLLM artifact. The
`rdllm-foundation-attribution-profile/v1` profile binds the provider card,
certification report, integration profile, discovery manifest, response envelope,
and L103 source-footer-delivery receipt to required HTTP headers, required JSON
fields, embedded proof objects, verifier commands, public paths, and fail-closed
client policies. A search engine, chat UI, agent runtime, or downstream model
gateway can reject attributed answers that omit the source-footer-delivery hash,
discovery hash, response-envelope hash, verifier endpoint, or embedded delivery
receipt. The profile is published after discovery at
`/.well-known/rdllm/foundation-attribution-profile.json`, avoiding a bootstrap
hash cycle while making the minimum model API surface independently testable
[@c2pa2026spec; @ietf2026scitt; @onweller2026cited; @li2026datadignity].

The L105 layer adds client attribution enforcement receipts. Provider-side
metadata still leaves a relying-party gap: a chat product, search result page,
agent runtime, or downstream model API can receive valid attribution metadata and
then strip a source label, ignore the verifier command, or render after a required
header is missing. `rdllm-client-attribution-enforcement/v1` is generated by the
client or gateway that displays the answer. It binds the observed L104 response
headers, observed payload hash, embedded response envelope, embedded
source-footer-delivery receipt, embedded foundation API profile, verifier commands,
source labels, and render-or-block decision. Missing required headers, missing
embedded proof objects, source-label drift, response-envelope drift, delivery
receipt failure, or private text leakage forces `block_unverified_attribution`.
This turns attribution from a provider declaration into a relying-party
enforcement claim that can be replayed by auditors and downstream platforms
[@aex2026framework; @openai2026provenance; @c2pa2026spec].

The L106 layer adds persistent memory provenance receipts. Long-running agents
and assistant products increasingly write durable memories, summaries, and profile
facts; those cells can become invisible sources for later answers unless they are
treated as derived works. `rdllm-persistent-memory-provenance/v1` binds each
memory write to source labels, upstream L105 and L103 proof hashes, license terms,
retention policy, allowed uses, royalty carry-forward rows, and delete tombstone
commitments. Each memory read is then bound to the client-enforced rendered
answer hash and must prove that carried source labels remain visible in the
verified footer. Missing origin proof, hidden carried labels, missing royalty
obligations, deleted-memory reuse, or private memory text leakage blocks direct
memory use. This layer directly operationalizes recent findings that durable
agent memory needs typed source roles, provenance-enriched retrieval, executable
evolution traces, cryptographic lineage, provenance-DAG credit assignment, and
snapshot-resilient attribution evidence rather than unstructured summaries alone
[@borro2026memori; @jin2026memir; @pham2026memorai; @deng2026memtrace;
@ouyang2026memlineage; @liao2026memq; @zhang2026memmark;
@modelContextProtocol2025; @aex2026framework].

The L107 layer adds private reasoning attribution receipts. The public footer can
be complete while hidden scratchpads, delegated reviewers, or router synthesis
used additional source influence that never appears in user-facing attribution.
`rdllm-private-reasoning-attribution/v1` represents each source-influenced
private step as a hash commitment with upstream artifact hashes, source labels,
memory/tool/delegation references, output commitments, client-rendered output
binding, and royalty rows. The raw chain-of-thought remains private; auditors can
replay commitments and fail the receipt if a hidden source label is missing from
the delivered footer, if a memory-influenced step lacks L106 provenance, if a
delegated step lacks model identity, or if private reasoning text leaks. This
extends agent/tool provenance and API-boundary attribution work into the private
reasoning boundary without requiring public disclosure of hidden traces
[@yu2026tracer; @aex2026framework; @openai2026agentsTracing].

The L108 layer adds post-training signal provenance receipts. Alignment and
evaluation rows can shape future model behavior while remaining outside ordinary
retrieval citations or training summaries: a preference label, reward score,
verifier outcome, critique, or RLVR trace may be derived from a source-grounded
answer and then reused as model-improving signal.
`rdllm-post-training-signal-provenance/v1` binds each signal row to the upstream L107 private-reasoning
receipt, the L91 model-lineage report, source labels, creator/work IDs, license
terms, reward and update commitments, synthetic disclosure, annotator or verifier
attestations, and royalty carry-forward rows. Verifiers reject unbound labels,
missing reward/update commitments, hidden synthetic inputs, absent attestations,
missing royalty coverage, tampering, or raw feedback/source/prompt leakage. This
turns recent post-training lineage findings into a settlement rule: alignment
signals are not attribution-free just because they are downstream of an answer
or reward model [@huang2026rlvr; @zhang2026tracingRoots; @qiu2026mps].

The L109 layer adds attribution bills of materials. A model release, foundation
API, dataset package, or downstream application can satisfy all response-time and
post-training rules yet still lose attribution at the supply-chain boundary if
source notices, license terms, proof hashes, and release artifacts are not carried
forward. `rdllm-attribution-bom/v1` packages model identity, source components,
notice hashes, license-term hashes, proof artifact hashes, L108 post-training
signal provenance, provider-card evidence, and the proof-dependency graph into a
CycloneDX-aligned AI/ML bill of materials. Verifiers reject missing source labels
from training or post-training provenance, absent notice or license-term hashes,
non-reproducible proof artifact hashes, missing proof graph binding, lower-than-L108
certification, tampering, or raw source/prompt/answer/feedback/notice/license/payment
leakage. This imports current AI bill-of-materials and ML-BOM standardization into
royalty attribution while addressing permissive-washing findings: attribution is
not only a payout row, but a release artifact that preserves notice and license
obligations across model, API, and application boundaries
[@cyclonedxMlbom2026; @hu2026aibom; @permissiveWashing2026].

The L110 layer adds creator attribution audit indexes. Aggregate transparency and
model-level bills of materials still leave a creator with a hard question: given a
creator ID, work ID, chunk ID, content hash, or source label, where exactly did
that work appear across answer footers, delivery receipts, model-release BOMs,
post-training signals, access leases, license transactions, model lineage,
training summaries, and payout receipts? `rdllm-creator-attribution-audit-index/v1`
answers that query through a signed query commitment, namespaced source identities,
artifact hash bindings, surface rows, creator-work rows, and non-inclusion
commitments. Verifiers reject uncommitted queries, unbound visible footer rows,
post-training source rows without royalty obligations, ABOM rows without notice
and license hashes, matched works without payout or obligation evidence, lower-than-L109
certification, tampering, or private query/prompt/source/feedback/notice/license/payment
leakage. This turns foundation-model transparency pressure and content-credential
practice into a creator-specific audit API rather than another aggregate
disclosure [@stanfordFmti2026; @c2pa2026spec; @californiaAB2013].

The L111 layer adds creator attribution audit federations. A creator-facing audit
index is necessary but insufficient when the same work appears across multiple
model providers, search products, agents, marketplaces, and downstream AI
clients. `rdllm-creator-attribution-audit-federation/v1` merges multiple L110
indexes under one query commitment, records participant provider IDs and index
hashes, enforces provider-scoped source-label namespaces, binds attribution
exchange or federation handshake artifacts, and emits conflict rows when the same
work resolves to inconsistent creator identities. Verifiers reject single-provider
"federations", duplicate provider IDs, query drift, unready L110 indexes,
unbound federation artifacts, duplicate federated rows, unresolved creator
identity conflicts, tampering, or private text leakage. This maps SCITT-style
transparent statements, W3C proof-carrying credentials, and OpenID
Federation-style trust chaining onto the source-attribution RAG finding that
source rows must be portable evidence, not provider-local citation strings
[@ietf2026scitt; @w3cVc2025; @w3cDataIntegrity; @openidFederation2024;
@nematov2025sourceAttributionRag].

The L112 layer adds creator audit federation transparency. Even a signed
cross-provider answer can equivocate if one creator sees one federation result,
another customer sees another, and no public log binds the query/provider set to
the answer hash. `rdllm-creator-attribution-audit-federation-transparency/v1`
publishes the L111 federation hash and every participant L110 index hash into
append-only transparency logs, proves inclusion against log roots, checks prefix
consistency, and rejects same-size split views or multiple federation answers for
the same query hash and provider-set hash. The report exposes hashes, counts, log
roots, and inclusion proofs rather than private query terms, prompts, answers,
source text, notices, licenses, or payment data. This extends Certificate
Transparency, SCITT, and witness-cosigned transparency practice to creator-facing
source attribution, making footer confidence independently monitorable rather
than dependent on a provider's private API response [@ietf2021ct; @ietf2026scitt;
@transparencyDevWitness; @nematov2025sourceAttributionRag].

The L113 layer adds creator audit transparency monitoring. Publication alone is
not enough: creators and auditors need a portable monitor that can scan L112 logs
for a creator query commitment, prove inclusion for every matching federation or
participant-index entry, report newly observed appearances since the prior run,
and fail closed when the same query/provider set produces contradictory answers.
`rdllm-creator-audit-transparency-monitor/v1` binds query hashes, provider-set
hashes, expected federation and participant-index hashes, L112 report hashes, log
roots, inclusion proofs, observation roots, and prior monitor continuity. It
discloses no raw creator/work query terms, prompts, answers, source text,
license text, or payment data. This responds directly to recent findings that
LLM source attributions and search-result citations may look plausible while
remaining externally unverifiable unless the source trail can be audited outside
the model response [@nematov2025sourceAttributionRag; @attributionCrisis2025;
@ietf2021ct; @ietf2026scitt].

The L114 layer adds private creator watch tokens. A public L113 monitor is useful
for accountability, but its query hashes can become stable creator/work lookup
keys. `rdllm-creator-audit-private-watch/v1` binds the L113 monitor hash and
replaces query hashes, provider-set hashes, federation hashes, participant-index
hashes, provider IDs, and observation identifiers with keyed watch tokens. The
reference implementation uses deterministic HMAC tokens for offline replay; a
production deployment can replace token creation with RFC 9497 VOPRF/POPRF,
private set intersection, or private-information-retrieval monitoring while
preserving the public artifact shape. This lets creators prove appearances to a
collective, auditor, or court without publishing a reusable watchlist [@ietf2023oprf;
@ietf2021ct; @nematov2025sourceAttributionRag].

The L115 layer adds deep-research citation audits. Deep research agents and
long-form answer systems can emit source-looking citations that pass superficial
link checks but fail relevance or factual-support checks. The
`rdllm-deep-research-citation-audit/v1` artifact parses the rendered answer,
records citation markers by position and context hash, resolves every marker to a
materialized source row, and requires each cited claim to bind resolved source
labels, source/quote hashes, relevance score, factual-support score, and accepted
support status. This makes the footer confidence signal claim-specific rather
than link-specific: reachable but unsupported citations fail before the answer can
claim grounded source attribution [@onweller2026cited; @nematov2025sourceAttributionRag;
@jain2026cuer].

The L116 layer adds source freshness audits. A source can be reachable and
supportive while still being stale for a current, latest, recent, rapidly
changing, or as-of claim. The `rdllm-source-freshness-audit/v1` artifact classifies
each claim's temporal requirement, binds selected sources to retrieved-at,
published-at or modified-at, valid-from/valid-until, content hash, and
source-version hash metadata, and verifies source-age and retrieval-lag limits at
answer time. Candidate rows prove that a fresher supported source was not ignored
unless it is materially weaker under the declared support margin. This upgrades
source footers from "where this answer says it came from" to "which source version
was temporally valid when the model answered" [@recencyqa2026; @vu2023freshllms;
@dynarag2026].

The L117 layer adds royalty-abuse audits. A platform-scale royalty system creates
new incentives to register synthetic sources, split duplicate works across linked
accounts, form reciprocal citation rings, or concentrate direct payout in a small
cluster while preserving plausible source footers. The
`rdllm-royalty-abuse-audit/v1` artifact therefore binds each settlement window to
source-farm risk scores, creator identity and linked-account signals,
duplicate-source clusters, high-risk creator/source relationships, direct-payout
shares, escrow actions, and upstream freshness, authenticity, confidence,
consensus, creator-audit-index, and valuation-method artifacts. Direct payout is
allowed only for low-risk rows; suspicious value must route to abuse-review escrow
with hash-only evidence. This turns attribution into a fraud-resistant settlement
gate, not just a display feature [@attributionCrisisLlmSearch2026; @gadWild2026].

The L118 layer adds consent revocation propagation audits. Rights changes are only
credible if they propagate beyond the registry: stale retrieval indexes, active
source leases, cached license transactions, previously generated source footers,
persistent memory, private reasoning traces, post-training signals, attribution
exchange manifests, downstream providers, and settlement queues can all preserve
old consent state. The `rdllm-consent-revocation-propagation/v1` artifact binds a
rights event to effective timestamps, changed policy hashes, required propagation
surfaces, surface-specific evidence hashes, downstream acknowledgements,
future-use denial probes, and preserved historical event hashes. Verification
fails if a revoked or newly prohibited work remains selectable, footer-visible as
verified, memory-reusable, exchange-relayable, downstream-unacknowledged, or
directly payable after the effective time. This turns opt-out and revocation from
a static compliance flag into an auditable runtime behavior [@w3cOdrl;
@iptc2025optout; @c2paTdmClarification; @ietf2026scitt].

The L119 layer adds evidence-force calibration. A source can be reachable,
licensed, fresh, and relevant while still not warranting the way an answer phrases
the claim. The `rdllm-evidence-force-calibration/v1` artifact therefore ranks each
cited claim and its supporting evidence across relation, modality, scope, temporal,
and numeric force. Verification fails if the answer raises weak evidence to
certainty, local evidence to universal scope, historical evidence to current
status, or approximate evidence to exact numbers. The artifact also binds the
signed citation-footer contract's visible claim rows to calibrated force rows, so
a displayed verified footer claim cannot bypass the warrant check. Verified
source footers and direct payout require calibrated claims; over-warranted claims
must be rewritten, blocked, warned, or escrowed. This turns citation support into
a claim-language control, not only a source-selection control [@forcebench2026;
@onweller2026cited; @nematov2025sourceAttributionRag].

The L120 layer adds warranted source footers. The
`rdllm-warranted-source-footer/v1` artifact converts L119's private replay into
visible footer labels: every displayed verified claim carries relation,
modality, scope, temporal, and numeric warrant labels, claim hashes, source
labels, span prefixes, settlement status, and proof roots. Verification fails
if a visible footer claim lacks L119 coverage, omits a required force axis, shows
an uncalibrated verified warrant, or leaks raw prompt, claim, evidence, source,
or payment text. This makes the answer footer explain what the cited source
actually warrants, not only which source was cited.

The L121 layer adds source-origin lineage. The
`rdllm-source-origin-lineage/v1` artifact prevents synthetic-source royalty
laundering by requiring each visible source row to prove its origin before direct
settlement. Human-origin rows need trusted origin attestation. Synthetic rows can
receive value only through active upstream creator lineage splits whose shares
conserve the source value. Unknown-origin or unattributed synthetic rows remain
visible to the user but route settlement to origin-review escrow. This closes the
gap where an AI-generated wrapper page could satisfy citation and warrant checks
while diverting royalties from original human creators [@syntheticSources2026;
@forcebench2026; @onweller2026cited; @nematov2025sourceAttributionRag].

The L122 layer adds evidence-preview footers. The
`rdllm-evidence-preview-footer/v1` artifact requires each verified visible claim
to publish a short permissioned evidence snippet, source URL, warrant label,
origin label, settlement label, and proof hash. It fails closed when a snippet is
missing, overlong, unlicensed, disconnected from L120 warrant rows, disconnected
from L121 origin rows, or leaks private prompt, answer, source, reasoning, or
payment text. This closes the last reader-facing gap: a footer no longer merely
names a source; it shows a bounded public evidence preview for the exact claim
while preserving copyright and privacy constraints [@forcebench2026;
@syntheticSources2026].

The L123 layer adds evidence-locator manifests. The
`rdllm-evidence-locator-manifest/v1` artifact requires each L122 preview snippet
to bind to an exact public resolver URL, resolver status, preview-row hash,
excerpt hash, and snapshot or text-fragment proof. It fails closed when the
locator is missing, unresolved, non-exact, source-URI mismatched, unsupported, not
bound to a snapshot or text fragment, or leaks private source, prompt, answer, or
payment text. This converts the footer from "source shown" to "precise passage
inspectable" while preserving the same copyright and privacy boundary
[@khajavi2026citecheck; @syntheticSources2026; @forcebench2026].

The L124 layer adds citation URL-health reports. The
`rdllm-citation-url-health/v1` artifact requires each L123 locator URL to be
classified as live, canonical-redirect live, content-addressed, DOI-resolved, or
archival link rot with snapshot evidence. Fabricated, never-seen, mismatched,
unarchived, or unverified locator URLs fail closed before footer display or
direct attribution. This layer is intentionally provider-neutral: OpenAI-,
Anthropic-, Google-, Meta-, and Mistral-style APIs can publish the same
resolver-evidence receipt even when their internal retrieval, ranking, and model
architectures differ [@khajavi2026citecheck; @syntheticSources2026].

The L125 layer adds composite foundation adapter reports. The
`rdllm-composite-foundation-adapter/v1` artifact makes the provider-neutral claim
testable against real API shapes: native OpenAI Responses, Anthropic Messages,
Google Gemini, Meta/Llama-style, Mistral, Cohere, xAI, Amazon Bedrock Converse,
Azure OpenAI Responses, and OpenAI-compatible response rows must map native
response identity, model identity, output hash, attribution headers, embedded
JSON proof fields, citation paths, tool paths, and final streaming-event hashes
to the same RDLLM response envelope, source-footer-delivery receipt, foundation
API profile, and citation URL-health report. Missing provider-family coverage,
native-output hash drift, missing proof mappings, missing citation/tool paths,
non-fail-closed policies, and private native response leakage fail closed
[@openaiResponsesApi2026; @anthropicMessagesApi2026; @googleGeminiApi2026;
@mistralApi2026; @llamaStackDocs2026; @cohereChatApi2026; @xaiApi2026;
@awsBedrockConverse2026; @azureOpenaiResponses2026; @aex2026framework].

The L126 layer adds foundation provider conformance matrices. The
`rdllm-foundation-provider-conformance/v1` artifact requires each supported
provider family to publish hash-only positive fixtures for synchronous response,
streaming response, tool use, citation or grounding metadata, URL-health binding,
structured proof fields, claim-support footers, and parametric-memory fallback,
plus negative fixtures for missing RDLLM headers, native-output hash mismatch,
unverified citation URLs, unsupported footer claims, streaming final-hash drift,
and private-text leakage. This converts attribution from a best-effort response
annotation into a provider-public proof obligation: a model API cannot claim L126
compatibility unless the public fixture matrix is adapter-backed, documented,
hash reproducible, and fail closed under citation, footer, and source-support
failure modes [@khajavi2026citecheck; @syntheticSources2026; @forcebench2026].

The L127 layer adds foundation runtime adapter receipts. The
`rdllm-foundation-runtime-adapter/v1` artifact takes one concrete native response
and verifies that its provider identity, native response ID, native output hash,
RDLLM attribution headers, embedded JSON proof fields, citation/tool paths,
stream-final event hashes, citation URL-health proof, and source-footer delivery
all normalize into the same response envelope. If any required runtime binding is
missing or drifted, the receipt records `block_display` instead of releasing a
user-visible answer. This is the executable boundary between provider conformance
and user trust: a provider can publish adapter rows and fixtures, but each answer
must still carry a reproducible response-time proof before it is shown
[@openaiResponsesApi2026; @anthropicMessagesApi2026; @googleGeminiApi2026;
@khajavi2026citecheck; @syntheticSources2026; @forcebench2026].

The L128 layer adds foundation runtime router receipts. The
`rdllm-foundation-runtime-router/v1` artifact covers the production layer where
real systems route among provider APIs, fallback models, latency tiers, and cost
tiers. It requires every candidate route to bind to an L125 adapter row, an L126
conformance row, route-health commitments, and a fail-closed fallback decision,
while the selected route must bind to a released L127 runtime adapter. This closes
the most likely adoption bypass: a product cannot satisfy attribution for one
provider response and then silently release an unverified fallback provider output
through routing logic. This design follows the same operational boundary surfaced
by GenAI telemetry standards, which distinguish requested models, response models,
providers, and proxied server paths, and by agent-provenance work that compares
actual execution provenance against authorized source/parameter paths. It also
keeps citation identity, factual support, and metadata faithfulness as separate
preconditions, so a router cannot treat provider-written citation strings as
sufficient proof [@otelGenaiSemconv; @authgraph2026; @panchigar2026scp;
@onweller2026cited; @khajavi2026citecheck; @nguyen2026attributionContract;
@shilpika2026probattr; @openaiResponsesApi2026; @anthropicMessagesApi2026;
@googleGeminiApi2026; @awsBedrockConverse2026; @azureOpenaiResponses2026;
@aex2026framework].

The L129 layer adds foundation model deployment attestations. The
`rdllm-foundation-model-deployment-attestation/v1` artifact covers the provider
backend behind the selected L128 route. It requires the route to bind to an
active deployment key, a provider-signed deployment statement, opaque
model/version commitments, request-projection hashes, native response IDs,
native output hashes, response-envelope hashes, and API-boundary attestation
hashes. This closes a second deployment bypass: a router can no longer show a
compliant route label while serving a different unaudited backend model or
compatibility-layer response. The artifact does not require providers to publish
raw weights or private requests; it requires hash commitments and signed boundary
evidence that independent verifiers can replay [@otelGenaiSemconv;
@aex2026framework; @panchigar2026scp; @authgraph2026;
@nguyen2026attributionContract].

The L130 layer adds universal composition receipts. The
`rdllm-universal-composition-receipt/v1` artifact covers the increasingly common
case where one public answer is assembled from multiple foundation-model
providers, fallback fragments, delegated model calls, or synthesis stages. Each
provider segment must match exactly one released L129 deployment attestation and
carry its provider family, model identity, native response ID, native output
hash, response-envelope hash, source-footer delivery hash, telemetry span
commitment, claim IDs, source labels, and provider weight. This closes the
composition bypass: a gateway can no longer merge provider outputs while
dropping a segment's footer, trace, deployment proof, or creator-value share.
The artifact remains privacy-preserving because it publishes hashes and segment
commitments rather than raw prompts, raw provider responses, or source text
[@aex2026framework; @otelGenaiSemconv; @panchigar2026scp].

The L131 layer adds universal composition settlement receipts. The
`rdllm-universal-composition-settlement/v1` artifact covers the economic closure
for an L130 composite answer. It requires each provider segment's source labels
and claim IDs to reconcile to source-entitlement rows, then recomputes creator
obligations from the segment weights, entitlement weights, revenue allocation
creator pool, and rights/claimant status. Licensed or verified rows may become
payable, unresolved rows route to escrow, and disputed, blocked, or revoked rows
route to hold. This closes the settlement bypass: a provider can no longer show a
grounded footer while clearing creator value against a different source set,
different claim IDs, or non-conserved creator pool. The artifact remains
auditable without exposing raw prompts, raw outputs, source text, customer data,
or payout-account details because it publishes hashes, aggregate totals, and
replayable obligation rows [@nguyen2026attributionContract; @wuehrl2026human;
@zhang2026a2aPayments; @panchigar2026scp].

The L132 layer adds universal foundation model contracts. The
`rdllm-universal-foundation-model-contract/v1` artifact converts the L125-L131
chain into a single provider-neutral adoption contract for foundation-model APIs,
brokers, gateways, and enterprise deployments. It requires every supported
provider family to be covered by the same composite adapter, conformance matrix,
runtime adapter, runtime router, deployment attestation, universal composition
receipt, universal composition settlement, integration profile, discovery
manifest, proof dependency graph, and public source footer. This closes the
adoption bypass: a provider can no longer claim RDLLM compatibility for one
model family while routing another family, fallback provider, or compatibility
layer outside the published proof chain. The artifact fails closed on missing
provider-family coverage, unbound route/deployment pairs, missing public
surfaces, unready settlement, footer-settlement mismatch, creator-pool drift, or
private prompt/output/source/payment leakage [@aex2026framework;
@otelGenaiSemconv; @nguyen2026attributionContract; @wuehrl2026human;
@zhang2026a2aPayments].

The L133 layer adds universal invocation guards. The
`rdllm-universal-invocation-guard/v1` artifact covers the last production bypass:
a broker, model gateway, SDK, or agent runtime could publish an L132 contract but
still call a native provider endpoint directly without admitting the invocation
through that contract. The guard requires the pre-call request to carry the L132
contract hash, runtime-router hash, deployment-attestation hash, selected route,
provider family, request-projection hash, response-binding hash, fail-closed
header, completed preflight checks, source-footer requirement, response-envelope
requirement, and GenAI telemetry for provider, requested model, served model,
contract hash, route, and request projection. This turns attribution from a
post-hoc footer into a pre-call release condition: missing headers, route drift,
request/response boundary drift, unready L132 contracts, source-footer omission,
telemetry drift, or private prompt/output/source/payment leakage block the native
provider call [@aex2026framework; @otelGenaiSemconv; @authgraph2026;
@zhang2026sear; @li2026datadignity; @liu2025olmotrace;
@wallat2025faithfulness].

The L134 layer adds universal invocation coverage. A per-call guard is necessary
but insufficient: a deployment could present a compliant L133 sample while other
provider calls bypass the gateway. The
`rdllm-universal-invocation-coverage/v1` artifact reconciles native provider
meter logs, gateway egress logs, L133 guard receipts, source-footer delivery
hashes, response-envelope hashes, provider invoice rows, gross revenue, and
creator-pool rows across a deployment window. Certification fails when a metered
native provider call lacks a guard, a guard lacks a meter event, duplicate guards
or meter rows appear for one invocation, gateway fields drift from the guard,
source-footer or response-envelope bindings are missing, invoice rows disagree
with meter rows, gross revenue is not conserved, creator-pool rows are missing,
or private prompt/output/source/customer/payment text leaks. This turns RDLLM
from "this call was guarded" into "all measured provider usage in this deployment
window was attributable, footer-bound, envelope-bound, and settlement-ready."

The L135 layer adds universal invocation witnesses. L134 proves the rows supplied
to the verifier reconcile; it does not by itself make omitted rows independently
detectable. The `rdllm-universal-invocation-witness/v1` artifact requires each
L134-covered native provider call to bind to a provider-signed usage receipt, an
independently observed egress event, and an independent witness quorum.
Certification fails when provider receipts are missing, egress observations are
missing, witness quorum is insufficient, provider/egress/witness fields drift
from coverage, duplicate receipt or egress identifiers appear, or private
prompt/output/source/customer/payment text leaks. This turns RDLLM from "all
reported provider usage reconciled" into "reported provider usage is backed by
external non-repudiation evidence."

The L136 layer adds universal content credentials. L135 proves a provider
invocation was non-repudiably observed, but it does not by itself make a copied,
exported, reposted, or embedded output carry one portable attribution object. The
`rdllm-universal-content-credential/v1` artifact binds the content subject and

incomplete, payout rows do not cover credited sources, output credentials or
durable signals are absent, provider invocation evidence is not non-repudiable, or
private prompt/output/source/payment text leaks. This turns RDLLM from "the API
response was attributed" into "the content itself carries a verifiable, portable
source and royalty credential" [@c2pa2026spec;
@lundstromimanov2026provenanceWatermarking; @golaszewski2026c2paShortfall;
@onweller2026cited; @li2026datadignity; @gurioli2026sourcetracker].

The L137 layer adds the universal RDLLM passport. L136 proves a released output
can carry its own source and royalty credential; it does not prove that every
supported foundation-model route, gateway, adapter, and public provider surface is
bound to the same attribution contract. The
`rdllm-universal-rdllm-passport/v1` artifact binds the L136 content credential to
the certification report and attestation, provider attribution card,

[@schreieder2025attributionCitationQuotation; @nematov2025sourceAttributionRag;
@choi2025citeguard; @li2026datadignity; @zhao2026wildcitations;
@w3cDataIntegrity; @otelGenaiSemconv; @modelContextProtocol2025].

The L138 layer adds the universal RDLLM adoption standard. L137 proves a
deployment passport is internally complete; it does not by itself tell an outside

content-provenance fragility into procurement controls: source footers must
resolve and verify, public verifier commands must exist, provider families must
be covered, conformance vectors must include negative mutations, C2PA/W3C/OTel/MCP

@c2pa2026spec; @golaszewski2026c2paShortfall; @w3cDataIntegrity;
@otelGenaiSemconv; @modelContextProtocol2025].

The L139 layer adds the universal RDLLM interop test kit. L138 defines what every

OpenAPI, HTTP/curl, Python, and TypeScript clients must fail closed; every
provider family must have sync/streaming/tool/citation fixtures; and mutation

signatures, rights-denied sources, hallucinated citations, unguarded calls, and
private-text leaks must block before display or settlement.

The L140 layer adds the universal context provenance bridge. Recent attributed
generation work shows that reliable citations need generation-time planning and
progress checks [@li2025thinkcite], RAG source-attribution work shows that
retrieved-document influence must be measured under practical replay constraints
[@nematov2025source], multimodal source-attribution benchmarks show that sources
may be images, regions, and mixed media rather than only text
[@song2026mavis], and large-scale audits show that hallucinated citations now
appear in real knowledge-production workflows [@zhao2026wildcitations]. At the
same time, MCP makes external tools and data sources first-class model context
surfaces [@modelContextProtocol2025], while C2PA, W3C integrity proofs, PROV, and
GPAI transparency/copyright policy provide reusable credential and disclosure
patterns [@c2pa2026spec; @w3cDataIntegrity; @w3c2013prov; @eu2025gpai]. RDLLM
therefore treats every context event from tools, retrieval, vector stores, files,
browser search, enterprise connectors, creator license endpoints, memory, and
media credentials as a pre-release attribution object: it must be authorized,
licensed, converted into source claims, projected into the footer when it supports
the answer, bound to agent-step attribution, and reconciled to royalty projection
before a context-influenced response is displayed.

The L141 layer adds the universal citation verification contract. L140 proves
that context paths can be carried through the response pipeline, but user trust
also depends on whether the visible citations are real, reachable, metadata-faithful,
claim-supporting, and matched to the evidence force claimed by the answer. Recent
work on wild citation audits, generated synthetic sources, attributed generation,
RAG source attribution, and evidence-force calibration shows that citation labels
can be fabricated, stale, unsupported, or overconfident even when an answer looks
well sourced [@zhao2026wildcitations; @syntheticSources2026; @li2025thinkcite;
@nematov2025source; @forcebench2026]. RDLLM therefore requires a signed
`rdllm-universal-citation-verification-contract/v1` artifact before the highest
certification level is reached. The artifact binds the source footer, response
envelope, source-verification report, citation URL health, evidence locator
manifest, answer-claim coverage, evidence-force calibration, source-confidence
report, warranted footer, rendered-attribution audit, trust registry, and L140
context bridge into one fail-closed contract: every displayed citation label must
resolve to a verified source, every cited claim must be covered by evidence, every
cited source must carry a royalty state, and hallucinated labels, metadata drift,
unsupported claims, inaccessible locators, stale snapshots, overclaimed force, and
private-text leaks must block certification.

The grounding-quality evaluator produces component scores for source accessibility,
citation integrity, evidence relevance, fact support, policy alignment, and payout
alignment. Its payout-alignment rule is intentionally strict: if a non-escrow source
receives money, that source must be visible in the footer and receipt. This prevents
a provider from paying hidden sources while presenting a narrower attribution story
to users or creators. The same evaluator treats `blocked_by_registry` as a valid
verdict only when payout alignment shows the creator pool was held in
registry-dispute escrow.

The attribution-gap evaluator turns that principle into a separate audit object. It
compares accessed chunks against footer-visible source references, non-escrow paid
shares, rights-conflict escrow, and registry-dispute escrow. A normal grounded event
must receive a `closed` verdict. A rights-blocked or registry-disputed event can
receive `escrowed`, but only when the withheld chunks are bound to the corresponding
escrow account. If a source is consumed without credit, cited without an access
trace, or paid while hidden from the user-facing footer, the verdict becomes
`open_gap`.

The prototype also exports a rights manifest aligned with ODRL, Croissant, SPDX, and
PROV vocabulary concepts. This manifest lists creators, works, chunk hashes,
permissions, prohibitions, royalty duties, jurisdiction constraints, and intended
uses, making license enforcement auditable before a model answers.

## 6. Training Royalties

Retrieval-time royalties are the highest-confidence layer because the system knows
which sources were supplied to the model. Base-model training attribution is weaker.
For that case, the mechanism uses a separate residual corpus royalty pool rather
than pretending that every answer has a precise training source:

1. Group licensed training works into cohorts.
2. Estimate each cohort's marginal value through Data Shapley, leave-one-out, TracIn,
   influence functions, Concept Influence, RISE-style readout sketches,
   zero-knowledge valuation proofs, or benchmark ablations.
3. Bind each valuation row to creator-license terms, training-content summary
   cohorts, valuation evidence hashes, and model-usage revenue rows.
4. Exclude visible/direct answer-attribution settlement rows from the residual
   pool to prevent double counting.
5. Route licensed, sufficiently confident rows to creator residual receipts; route
   unlicensed, revoked, missing-evidence, or low-confidence rows to escrow.
6. Publish aggregate training-content summaries, residual corpus royalty reports,
   and valuation-method audit reports while preserving private prompts, outputs,
   source text, validation data, customer records, benchmark rows, and payment
   details.

This keeps the system honest about uncertainty: direct provenance gets event-level
source footers and direct royalties; training influence gets statistically
estimated residual royalties only after rights, valuation, cap, receipt, and
conservation checks pass, and only after the valuation method itself passes
benchmark, anti-document, duplicate-guard, calibration, stability, and privacy
audits.

## 7. Governance

A credible royalty system needs dispute and licensing rules:

- consent and opt-in registration
- portable ownership attestations, preferably interoperable with verifiable
  credential patterns
- portable attribution credentials and PROV-shaped answer provenance graphs
- clear license scopes for training, retrieval, display, and derivative use
- machine-readable allowed uses, prohibited uses, jurisdictions, minimum royalty
  rates, and revocation state
- duplicate and near-duplicate work detection
- registry-dispute escrow for contested ownership claims
- verifiable release reports when contested ownership claims are resolved
- creator appeal workflows
- fraud penalties
- minimum payout thresholds
- public transparency reports
- private audit exports

Collective management can help where individual licensing is too costly, but the
system should preserve direct registration for creators who want explicit control.

## 8. Boundary Conditions

The prototype covers traceable attribution channels: retrieval use, generated text
overlap, explicit citations, training-value priors, unattributed escrow,
rights-conflict escrow, and registry-dispute escrow. It does not make legal
ownership decisions from a hash alone; ownership still depends on registration,
contracts, dispute handling, and applicable law. The registry layer proves a more
limited but important property: open duplicate claims do not receive automatic
direct settlement. The settlement layer proves a second property: once ownership is
resolved, held value can be released without mutating the original receipt. The
interoperability layer proves a third property: attribution facts can be moved into
external audit systems without losing hash and proof bindings. Style-only
similarity without copied expression remains a governance and policy question, so
the system treats it as claim evidence rather than automatic payout proof.

## 9. Conclusion

Royalty-Driven LLMs offer a concrete route from creator-rights theory to software.
The mechanism adapts usage-based revenue sharing to AI usage events, combines it with
provenance, text matching, ownership-claim checking, and data-valuation research,
and demonstrates a replayable payout, provenance, credential, and settlement ledger
for any AI system that uses registered content.
